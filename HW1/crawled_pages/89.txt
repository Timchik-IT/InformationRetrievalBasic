<p>A <b>commodity market</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Market_(economics)" title="Market (economics)">market</a> that trades in the <a href="/wiki/Primary_sector_of_the_economy" class="mw-redirect" title="Primary sector of the economy">primary economic sector</a> rather than manufactured products.  The primary sector includes agricultural products, energy products, and metals. Soft commodities may be perishable and harvested, while hard <a href="/wiki/Commodities" class="mw-redirect" title="Commodities">commodities</a> are usually mined, such as <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crude_oil" class="mw-redirect" title="Crude oil">oil</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Futures_contract" title="Futures contract">Futures contracts</a> are the oldest way of investing in commodities.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="dubious, should clarify the affirmation (December 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Commodity markets can include physical trading and derivatives trading using <a href="/wiki/Spot_price" class="mw-redirect" title="Spot price">spot prices</a>, <a href="/wiki/Forward_contract" title="Forward contract">forwards</a>, <a href="/wiki/Futures_contract" title="Futures contract">futures</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Option_(finance)" title="Option (finance)">options</a> on futures.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="&quot;can include&quot; Physical VS Paper (December 2020)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Farmers have used a simple form of derivative trading in the commodities market for centuries for price risk management.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-NFA2006&#95;2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NFA2006-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Derivative_(finance)" title="Derivative (finance)">financial derivative</a> is a financial instrument whose value is derived from a commodity termed an <a href="/wiki/Underlying" class="mw-redirect" title="Underlying">underlier</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-washingtonpost21apr2010&#95;3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-washingtonpost21apr2010-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Derivatives are either <a href="/wiki/Exchange-traded_derivative_contract" title="Exchange-traded derivative contract">exchange-traded</a> or <a href="/wiki/Over-the-counter_(finance)" title="Over-the-counter (finance)">over-the-counter</a> (OTC). An increasing number of derivatives are traded via <a href="/wiki/Clearing_house_(finance)" title="Clearing house (finance)">clearing houses</a> some with <a href="/wiki/Central_counterparty_clearing" title="Central counterparty clearing">central counterparty clearing</a>, which provide clearing and settlement services on a futures exchange, as well as off-exchange in the OTC market.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Derivatives such as futures contracts, <a href="/wiki/Swap_(finance)" title="Swap (finance)">Swaps</a> (1970s–), and Exchange-traded Commodities (ETC) (2003–) have become the primary trading instruments in commodity markets. Futures are traded on regulated <a href="/wiki/Commodities_exchange" class="mw-redirect" title="Commodities exchange">commodities exchanges</a>. Over-the-counter (OTC) contracts are "privately negotiated bilateral contracts entered into between the contracting parties directly".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-DerivativesCanada&#95;5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DerivativesCanada-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-bloomberg18Jul2010&#95;6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bloomberg18Jul2010-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Exchange-traded_fund" title="Exchange-traded fund">Exchange-traded funds</a> (ETFs) began to feature commodities in 2003. Gold ETFs are based on "electronic gold" that does not entail the ownership of physical bullion, with its added costs of insurance and storage in repositories such as the <a href="/wiki/London_bullion_market" title="London bullion market">London bullion market</a>. According to the <a href="/wiki/World_Gold_Council" title="World Gold Council">World Gold Council</a>, ETFs allow investors to be exposed to the gold market without the risk of price <a href="/wiki/Volatility_(finance)" title="Volatility (finance)">volatility</a> associated with gold as a physical commodity.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-GoldMutualVSETF&#95;7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GoldMutualVSETF-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-commodityETF&#95;8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-commodityETF-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>notes 1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="History">History</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commodity_market&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Commodity-based money and commodity markets in a crude early form are believed to have originated in <a href="/wiki/Sumer" title="Sumer">Sumer</a> between 4500 BC and 4000 BC. Sumerians first used <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clay</a> tokens sealed in a clay vessel, then <a href="/wiki/Sumer#Mathematics" title="Sumer">clay writing tablets</a> to represent the amount—for example, the number of goats, to be delivered.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-forbesindia16jan2013&#95;10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forbesindia16jan2013-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> These promises of time and date of delivery resemble <a href="/wiki/Futures_contract" title="Futures contract">futures contract</a>.
</p><p>Early civilizations variously used pigs, rare seashells, or other items as <a href="/wiki/Commodity_money" title="Commodity money">commodity money</a>. Since that time traders have sought ways to simplify and standardize trade contracts.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-books.google.com&#95;12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-books.google.com-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">Gold</a> and <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a> markets evolved in classical civilizations. At first, the precious metals were valued for their beauty and intrinsic worth and were associated with royalty.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-books.google.com&#95;12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-books.google.com-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In time, they were used for trading and were exchanged for other goods and commodities, or for payments of labor.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Gold, measured out, then became money. Gold's scarcity, its unique density and the way it could be easily melted, shaped, and measured made it a natural trading asset.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Beginning in the late 10th century, commodity markets grew as a mechanism for allocating goods, labor, land and capital across Europe. Between the late 11th and the late 13th century, English urbanization, regional specialization, expanded and improved infrastructure, the increased use of coinage and the proliferation of markets and fairs were evidence of commercialization.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-comarhistoryHolland2010&#95;16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-comarhistoryHolland2010-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The spread of markets is illustrated by the 1466 installation of reliable scales in the villages of Sloten and Osdorp so villagers no longer had to travel to Haarlem or Amsterdam to weigh their locally produced cheese and butter.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-comarhistoryHolland2010&#95;16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-comarhistoryHolland2010-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam_Stock_Exchange" class="mw-redirect" title="Amsterdam Stock Exchange">Amsterdam Stock Exchange</a>, often cited as the first stock exchange, originated as a market for the exchange of commodities. Early trading on the Amsterdam Stock Exchange often involved the use of very sophisticated contracts, including short sales, forward contracts, and options. "Trading took place at the Amsterdam Bourse, an open aired venue, which was created as a commodity exchange in 1530 and rebuilt in 1608. Commodity exchanges themselves were a relatively recent invention, existing in only a handful of cities."<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Stringham&#95;17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stringham-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In 1864, in the United States, wheat, corn, cattle, and pigs were widely traded using standard instruments on the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Board_of_Trade" title="Chicago Board of Trade">Chicago Board of Trade</a> (CBOT), the world's oldest futures and options exchange. Other food commodities were added to the <a href="/wiki/Commodity_Exchange_Act" title="Commodity Exchange Act">Commodity Exchange Act</a> and traded through CBOT in the 1930s and 1940s, expanding the list from grains to include rice, mill feeds, butter, eggs, Irish potatoes and soybeans.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Successful commodity markets require broad consensus on product variations to make each commodity acceptable for trading, such as the purity of gold in bullion.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Classical civilizations built complex global markets trading gold or silver for spices, cloth, wood and weapons, most of which had standards of quality and timeliness.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Through the 19th century "the exchanges became effective spokesmen for, and innovators of, improvements in transportation, warehousing, and financing, which paved the way to expanded interstate and international trade."<sup id="cite&#95;ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Reputation and clearing became central concerns, and states that could handle them most effectively developed powerful financial centers.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-HistoryCFuturesMarkham1987&#95;22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HistoryCFuturesMarkham1987-22"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Commodity_price_index">Commodity price index</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commodity_market&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Commodity price index"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>In 1934, the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Labor_Statistics" title="Bureau of Labor Statistics">Bureau of Labor Statistics</a> began the computation of a daily <a href="/wiki/Commodity_price_index" title="Commodity price index">Commodity price index</a> that became available to the public in 1940. By 1952, the Bureau of Labor Statistics issued a Spot Market Price Index that measured the price movements of "22 sensitive basic commodities whose markets are presumed to be among the first to be influenced by changes in economic conditions. As such, it serves as one early indication of impending changes in business activity."<sup id="cite&#95;ref-CRBspotindex2006&#95;23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRBspotindex2006-23"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Commodity_index_fund">Commodity index fund</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commodity_market&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Commodity index fund"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Commodity_index_fund" title="Commodity index fund">commodity index fund</a> is a fund whose assets are invested in financial instruments based on or linked to a commodity index. In just about every case the index is in fact a Commodity Futures Index. The first such index was the Dow Jones Commodity Index, which began in 1933.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>23<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>  The first practically investable commodity futures index was the <a href="/wiki/Goldman_Sachs_Commodity_Index" class="mw-redirect" title="Goldman Sachs Commodity Index">Goldman Sachs Commodity Index</a>, created in 1991,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-bub&#95;25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bub-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and known as the "GSCI". The next was the Dow Jones AIG Commodity Index. It differed from the GSCI primarily in the weights allocated to each commodity. The DJ AIG had mechanisms to periodically limit the weight of any one commodity and to remove commodities whose weights became too small. After <a href="/wiki/AIG" class="mw-redirect" title="AIG">AIG</a>'s financial problems in 2008 the Index rights were sold to <a href="/wiki/UBS" title="UBS">UBS</a> and it is now known as the DJUBS index. Other commodity indices include the Reuters / CRB index (which is the old CRB Index as re-structured in 2005) and the Rogers Index.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Cash_commodity">Cash commodity</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commodity_market&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cash commodity"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Cash commodities or "actuals" refer to the physical goods—e.g., wheat, corn, soybeans, crude oil, gold, silver—that someone is buying/selling/trading as distinguished from derivatives.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-NFA2006&#95;2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NFA2006-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Electronic_commodities_trading">Electronic commodities trading</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commodity_market&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Electronic commodities trading"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>In traditional <a href="/wiki/Stock_exchange#Major_stock_exchanges" title="Stock exchange">stock market exchanges</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">New York Stock Exchange</a> (NYSE), most trading activity took place in the <a href="/wiki/Trading_floor" class="mw-redirect" title="Trading floor">trading pits</a> in face-to-face interactions between brokers and dealers in <a href="/wiki/Open_outcry" title="Open outcry">open outcry trading</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-highfrequencytrading2011&#95;26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-highfrequencytrading2011-26"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>25<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In 1992 the  <a href="/wiki/Financial_Information_eXchange" title="Financial Information eXchange">Financial Information eXchange</a> (FIX) protocol was introduced, allowing international real-time exchange of information regarding market transactions. The <a href="/wiki/U.S._Securities_and_Exchange_Commission" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission">U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission</a> ordered U.S. stock markets to convert from the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_units" title="Imperial units">fractional system</a> to a <a href="/wiki/Decimal" title="Decimal">decimal system</a> by April 2001. <a href="/wiki/Metrification" class="mw-redirect" title="Metrification">Metrification</a>, conversion from the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_units" title="Imperial units">imperial system of measurement</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Metric_system" title="Metric system">metrical</a>, increased throughout the 20th century.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-decimal&#95;27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-decimal-27"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>26<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Eventually FIX-compliant interfaces were adopted globally by commodity exchanges using the FIX Protocol.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FIXGlobal2011&#95;28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FIXGlobal2011-28"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>27<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In 2001 the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Board_of_Trade" title="Chicago Board of Trade">Chicago Board of Trade</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Mercantile_Exchange" title="Chicago Mercantile Exchange">Chicago Mercantile Exchange</a> (later merged into the CME group, the world's largest futures exchange company)<sup id="cite&#95;ref-decimal&#95;27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-decimal-27"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>26<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> launched their FIX-compliant interface.
</p><p>By 2011, the <a href="/wiki/Alternative_trading_system" title="Alternative trading system">alternative trading system</a> (ATS) of <a href="/wiki/Electronic_communication_network" title="Electronic communication network">electronic trading</a> featured computers buying and selling without human dealer intermediation. <a href="/wiki/High-frequency_trading" title="High-frequency trading">High-frequency trading</a> (HFT) algorithmic trading, had almost phased out "dinosaur floor-traders".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-highfrequencytrading2011&#95;26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-highfrequencytrading2011-26"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>25<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>notes 2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Complexity_and_interconnectedness_of_global_market">Complexity and interconnectedness of global market</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commodity_market&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Complexity and interconnectedness of global market"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The robust growth of <a href="/wiki/Emerging_markets" class="mw-redirect" title="Emerging markets">emerging market economies</a> (EMEs, such as Brazil, Russia, India, and China), beginning in the 1990s, "propelled commodity markets into a supercycle". The size and diversity of commodity markets expanded internationally,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-BankofCanada25sept2012&#95;30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BankofCanada25sept2012-30"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>28<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Pension_fund" title="Pension fund">pension funds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_wealth_fund" title="Sovereign wealth fund">sovereign wealth funds</a> started allocating more capital to commodities, in order to <a href="/wiki/Diversification_(finance)" title="Diversification (finance)">diversify</a> into an asset class with less exposure to currency depreciation.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>29<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In 2012, as emerging-market economies slowed down, commodity prices peaked and started to decline. From 2005 through 2013, energy and metals' <a href="/wiki/Real_prices_and_ideal_prices" class="mw-redirect" title="Real prices and ideal prices">real prices</a> remained well above their long-term averages. In 2012, real <a href="/wiki/Food_prices" title="Food prices">food prices</a> were their highest since 1982.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-BankofCanada25sept2012&#95;30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BankofCanada25sept2012-30"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>28<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The price of gold bullion fell dramatically on 12 April 2013 and analysts frantically sought explanations. Rumors spread that the <a href="/wiki/European_Central_Bank" title="European Central Bank">European Central Bank</a> (ECB) would force <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a> to sell its gold reserves in response to its <a href="/wiki/2012%E2%80%932013_Cypriot_financial_crisis" title="2012–2013 Cypriot financial crisis">financial crisis</a>. Major banks such as <a href="/wiki/Goldman_Sachs" title="Goldman Sachs">Goldman Sachs</a> began immediately to short gold bullion. Investors scrambled to liquidate their <a href="/wiki/Exchange-traded_fund" title="Exchange-traded fund">exchange-traded funds</a> (ETFs)<sup id="cite&#95;ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>notes 3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Margin_(finance)" title="Margin (finance)">margin call selling</a> accelerated. George Gero, precious metals commodities expert at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Bank_of_Canada" title="Royal Bank of Canada">Royal Bank of Canada</a> (RBC) Wealth Management section reported that he had not seen selling of gold bullion as panicked as this in his forty years in commodity markets.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Forbes15apr2013&#95;33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Forbes15apr2013-33"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>30<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The earliest commodity exchange-traded fund (ETFs), such as <a href="/wiki/SPDR_Gold_Shares" title="SPDR Gold Shares">SPDR Gold Shares</a> <a href="/wiki/NYSE_Arca" title="NYSE Arca">NYSE&#160;Arca</a>:&#160;<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.nyse.com/quote/ARCX:GLD">GLD</a> and <a href="/wiki/IShares" title="IShares">iShares</a> Silver Trust <a href="/wiki/NYSE_Arca" title="NYSE Arca">NYSE&#160;Arca</a>:&#160;<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.nyse.com/quote/ARCX:SLV">SLV</a>, actually owned the physical commodities. Similar to these are <a href="/wiki/NYSE_Arca" title="NYSE Arca">NYSE&#160;Arca</a>:&#160;<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.nyse.com/quote/ARCX:PALL">PALL</a> (<a href="/wiki/Palladium" title="Palladium">palladium</a>) and <a href="/wiki/NYSE_Arca" title="NYSE Arca">NYSE&#160;Arca</a>:&#160;<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.nyse.com/quote/ARCX:PPLT">PPLT</a> (<a href="/wiki/Platinum" title="Platinum">platinum</a>). However, most Exchange Traded Commodities (ETCs) implement a <a href="/wiki/Futures_trading" class="mw-redirect" title="Futures trading">futures trading</a> strategy. At the time Russian Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Dmitry_Medvedev" title="Dmitry Medvedev">Dmitry Medvedev</a> warned that Russia could sink into recession. He argued that "We live in a dynamic, fast-developing world. It is so global and so complex that we sometimes cannot keep up with the changes". Analysts have claimed that Russia's economy is overly dependent on commodities.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FinancialTimes17apr2013&#95;34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FinancialTimes17apr2013-34"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>31<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Contracts_in_the_commodity_market">Contracts in the commodity market</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commodity_market&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Contracts in the commodity market"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Spot_contract" title="Spot contract">Spot contract</a> is an agreement where delivery and payment either takes place immediately, or with a short lag. Physical trading normally involves a visual inspection and is carried out in physical <a href="/wiki/Market_(place)" class="mw-redirect" title="Market (place)">markets</a> such as a <a href="/wiki/Farmers%27_market" title="Farmers&#39; market">farmers market</a>. <a href="/wiki/Derivatives_market" title="Derivatives market">Derivatives markets</a>, on the other hand, require the existence of agreed standards so that trades can be made without visual inspection.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Standardization">Standardization</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commodity_market&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Standardization"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>

<p>US <a href="/wiki/Soybean" title="Soybean">soybean</a> futures do not qualify as "standard grade" if they are "GMO or a mixture of GMO and Non-GMO No. 2 yellow soybeans of Indiana, Ohio and Michigan origin produced in the U.S.A. (Non-screened, stored in silo)". They are of "deliverable grade" if they are "GMO or a mixture of GMO and Non-GMO No. 2 yellow soybeans of Iowa, Illinois and Wisconsin origin produced in the U.S.A. (Non-screened, stored in silo)".  Note the distinction between states, and the need to clearly mention their status as GMO (<a href="/wiki/Genetically_modified_organism" title="Genetically modified organism">genetically modified organism</a>) which makes them unacceptable to most <a href="/wiki/Organic_farming" title="Organic farming">organic</a> food buyers.
</p><p>Similar specifications apply for cotton, orange juice, cocoa, sugar, wheat, corn, barley, <a href="/wiki/Pork_belly" title="Pork belly">pork bellies</a>, milk, feed stuffs, fruits, vegetables, other grains, other beans, hay, other livestock, meats, poultry, eggs, or any other commodity which is so traded.
</p><p>Standardization has also occurred technologically, as the use of the FIX Protocol by commodities exchanges has allowed trade messages to be sent, received and processed in the same format as stocks or equities. This process began in 2001 when the Chicago Mercantile Exchange launched a FIX-compliant interface that was adopted by commodity exchanges around the world.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FIXGlobal2011&#95;28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FIXGlobal2011-28"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>27<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Derivatives">Derivatives</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commodity_market&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Derivatives"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Derivative_(finance)" title="Derivative (finance)">Derivatives</a> evolved from simple commodity future contracts into a diverse group of financial instruments that apply to every kind of asset, including mortgages, insurance and many more. Futures contracts, <a href="/wiki/Swap_(finance)" title="Swap (finance)">Swaps</a> (1970s–), Exchange-traded Commodities (ETC) (2003–), forward contracts, etc. are examples. They can be traded through formal exchanges or through Over-the-counter (OTC). Commodity market derivatives unlike credit default derivatives, for example, are secured by the physical assets or commodities.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-washingtonpost21apr2010&#95;3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-washingtonpost21apr2010-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Forward_contracts">Forward contracts</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commodity_market&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Forward contracts"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Forward_contract" title="Forward contract">forward contract</a> is an agreement between two parties to exchange at a fixed future date a given quantity of a commodity for a specific price defined when the contract is finalized. The fixed price is also called <a href="/wiki/Forward_price" title="Forward price">forward price</a>. Such forward contracts began as a way of reducing pricing risk in food and agricultural product markets. By agreeing in advance on a price for a future delivery, farmers were able protect their output against a possible fall of market prices and in contrast buyers were able to protect themselves against a possible rise of market prices.
</p><p>Forward contracts, for example, were used for rice in seventeenth century Japan.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Futures_contract">Futures contract</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commodity_market&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Futures contract"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Futures_contract" title="Futures contract">Futures</a> contracts are standardized forward contracts that are transacted through an exchange. In futures contracts the buyer and the seller stipulate product, grade, quantity and location and leaving price as the only variable.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>32<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Agricultural futures contracts are the oldest, in use in the United States for more than 170 years.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>33<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Modern futures agreements, began in Chicago in the 1840s, with the appearance of <a href="/wiki/Grain_elevators" class="mw-redirect" title="Grain elevators">grain elevators</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>34<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Chicago, centrally located, emerged as the hub between Midwestern farmers and east coast consumer population centers.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Call_options">Call options</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commodity_market&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Call options"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>In a <a href="/wiki/Call_option" title="Call option">call option</a> <a href="/wiki/Counterparty" title="Counterparty">counterparties</a> enter into a financial contract option where the buyer purchases the right but not the obligation to buy an agreed quantity of a particular commodity or financial instrument (the underlying) from the seller of the option at a certain time (the expiration date) for a certain price (the <a href="/wiki/Strike_price" title="Strike price">strike price</a>). The seller (or "writer") is obligated to sell the commodity or financial instrument should the buyer so decide. The buyer pays a fee (called a premium) for this right.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>35<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Swaps">Swaps</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commodity_market&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Swaps"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Swap_(finance)" title="Swap (finance)">swap</a> is a derivative in which counterparties exchange the cash flows of one party's financial instrument for those of the other party's financial instrument. They were introduced in the 1970s.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-GlobalFinance2003&#95;39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GlobalFinance2003-39"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>36<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-JournalFuturesMarkets&#95;40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JournalFuturesMarkets-40"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>37<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Exchange-traded_commodities_(ETCs)"><span id="Exchange-traded_commodities_.28ETCs.29"></span>Exchange-traded commodities (ETCs)</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commodity_market&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Exchange-traded commodities (ETCs)"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Exchange-traded_product" title="Exchange-traded product">Exchange-traded product</a></div>
<p>Exchange-traded commodity is a term used for commodity ETFs (which are funds) or commodity <a href="/wiki/Exchange-traded_note" title="Exchange-traded note">exchange-traded notes</a> (which are notes). These track the performance of an underlying commodity index including total return indices based on a single commodity. They are similar to ETFs and traded and settled exactly like stock funds. ETCs have <a href="/wiki/Market_maker" title="Market maker">market maker</a> support with guaranteed liquidity, enabling investors to easily invest in commodities.
</p><p>They were introduced in 2003.
</p><p>At first, only professional institutional investors had access, but online exchanges opened some ETC markets to almost anyone. ETCs were introduced partly in response to the tight supply of commodities in 2000, combined with record low inventories and increasing demand from emerging markets such as China and India.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-ETC&#95;41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ETC-41"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>38<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Prior to the introduction of ETCs, by the 1990s ETFs pioneered by <a href="/wiki/Barclays_Global_Investors" class="mw-redirect" title="Barclays Global Investors">Barclays Global Investors</a> (BGI) revolutionized the mutual funds industry.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-ETC&#95;41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ETC-41"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>38<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> By the end of December 2009 BGI assets hit an all-time high of $1 trillion.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>39<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Gold was the first commodity to be securitised through an ETF in the early 1990s, but it was not available for trade until 2003.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-ETC&#95;41-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ETC-41"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>38<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The idea of a Gold ETF was first officially conceptualised by <a href="/wiki/Benchmark_Asset_Management_Company_Private_Ltd" class="mw-redirect" title="Benchmark Asset Management Company Private Ltd">Benchmark Asset Management Company Private Ltd</a> in India, when they filed a proposal with the <a href="/wiki/Securities_and_Exchange_Board_of_India" title="Securities and Exchange Board of India">Securities and Exchange Board of India</a> in May 2002.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>40<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The first gold exchange-traded fund was <a href="/wiki/Gold_Bullion_Securities" class="mw-redirect" title="Gold Bullion Securities">Gold Bullion Securities</a> launched on the ASX in 2003, and the first <a href="/wiki/Silver_exchange-traded_fund" class="mw-redirect" title="Silver exchange-traded fund">silver exchange-traded fund</a> was iShares Silver Trust launched on the NYSE in 2006. As of November 2010 a commodity ETF, namely <a href="/wiki/SPDR_Gold_Shares" title="SPDR Gold Shares">SPDR Gold Shares</a>, was the second-largest ETF by market capitalization.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-etfdb1&#95;44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-etfdb1-44"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>41<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Generally, commodity ETFs are index funds tracking non-security <a href="/wiki/Index_(economics)" title="Index (economics)">indices</a>. Because they do not invest in securities, commodity ETFs are not regulated as investment companies under the <a href="/wiki/Investment_Company_Act_of_1940" title="Investment Company Act of 1940">Investment Company Act of 1940</a> in the United States, although their public offering is subject to SEC review and they need an SEC <a href="/wiki/No-action_letter" title="No-action letter">no-action letter</a> under the <a href="/wiki/Securities_Exchange_Act_of_1934" title="Securities Exchange Act of 1934">Securities Exchange Act of 1934</a>. They may, however, be subject to regulation by the <a href="/wiki/Commodity_Futures_Trading_Commission" title="Commodity Futures Trading Commission">Commodity Futures Trading Commission</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>42<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>43<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The earliest commodity ETFs, such as SPDR Gold Shares <a href="/wiki/NYSE_Arca" title="NYSE Arca">NYSE&#160;Arca</a>:&#160;<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.nyse.com/quote/ARCX:GLD">GLD</a> and <a href="/wiki/IShares" title="IShares">iShares</a> Silver Trust <a href="/wiki/NYSE_Arca" title="NYSE Arca">NYSE&#160;Arca</a>:&#160;<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.nyse.com/quote/ARCX:SLV">SLV</a>, actually owned the physical commodity (e.g., gold and silver bars). Similar to these are <a href="/wiki/NYSE_Arca" title="NYSE Arca">NYSE&#160;Arca</a>:&#160;<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.nyse.com/quote/ARCX:PALL">PALL</a> (palladium) and <a href="/wiki/NYSE_Arca" title="NYSE Arca">NYSE&#160;Arca</a>:&#160;<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.nyse.com/quote/ARCX:PPLT">PPLT</a> (platinum). However, most ETCs implement a futures trading strategy, which may produce quite different results from owning the commodity.
</p><p>Commodity ETFs trade provide exposure to an increasing range of commodities and commodity indices, including energy, metals, <a href="/wiki/Soft_commodity" title="Soft commodity">softs</a> and agriculture. Many commodity funds, such as oil roll so-called front-month futures contracts from month to month. This provides exposure to the commodity, but subjects the investor to risks involved in different prices along the <i>term structure</i>, such as a high cost to roll.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-GoldMutualVSETF&#95;7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GoldMutualVSETF-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-commodityETF&#95;8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-commodityETF-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>ETCs in China and India gained in importance due to those countries' emergence as commodities consumers and producers. China accounted for more than 60% of exchange-traded commodities in 2009, up from 40% the previous year. The global volume of ETCs increased by a 20% in 2010, and 50% since 2008, to around 2.5 billion million contracts.{{<sup id="cite&#95;ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>44<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>}}
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Over-the-counter_(OTC)_commodities_derivatives"><span id="Over-the-counter_.28OTC.29_commodities_derivatives"></span>Over-the-counter (OTC) commodities derivatives</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commodity_market&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Over-the-counter (OTC) commodities derivatives"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Over-the-counter_(finance)" title="Over-the-counter (finance)">Over-the-counter</a> (OTC) commodities derivatives trading originally involved two parties, without an <a href="/wiki/Exchange_(organized_market)" title="Exchange (organized market)">exchange</a>.  Exchange trading offers greater transparency and regulatory protections. In an OTC trade, the price is not generally made public. OTC commodities derivatives are higher risk but may also lead to higher profits.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>45<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Between 2007 and 2010, global physical exports of commodities fell by 2%, while the outstanding value of OTC commodities derivatives declined by two-thirds as investors reduced risk following a five-fold increase in the previous three years.
</p><p>Money under management more than doubled between 2008 and 2010 to nearly $380 billion. Inflows into the sector totaled over $60 billion in 2010, the second-highest year on record, down from $72 billion the previous year. The bulk of funds went into precious metals and energy products. The growth in prices of many commodities in 2010 contributed to the increase in the value of commodities funds under management.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>46<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Commodities_exchange">Commodities exchange</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commodity_market&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Commodities exchange"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Commodities_exchange" class="mw-redirect" title="Commodities exchange">Commodities exchange</a></div>
<p>A commodities exchange is an exchange where various commodities and derivatives are traded. Most commodity markets across the world trade in agricultural products and other raw materials (like wheat, barley, sugar, maize, cotton, <a href="/wiki/Cocoa_bean" title="Cocoa bean">cocoa</a>, coffee, milk products, pork bellies, oil, metals, etc.) and contracts based on them. These contracts can include spot prices, forwards, futures and options on futures. Other sophisticated products may include interest rates, environmental instruments, swaps, or freight contracts.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-NFA2006&#95;2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NFA2006-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Energy">Energy</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commodity_market&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Energy"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Energy commodities include <a href="/wiki/Crude_oil" class="mw-redirect" title="Crude oil">crude oil</a> particularly <a href="/wiki/West_Texas_Intermediate" title="West Texas Intermediate">West Texas Intermediate</a> (WTI) crude oil and <a href="/wiki/Brent_Crude" title="Brent Crude">Brent crude oil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heating_oil" title="Heating oil">heating oil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Purified_terephthalic_acid" class="mw-redirect" title="Purified terephthalic acid">purified terephthalic acid</a>. <a href="/wiki/Fuel_hedging" title="Fuel hedging">Hedging</a> is a common practice for these commodities.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading4"><h4 id="Crude_oil_and_natural_gas">Crude oil and natural gas</h4><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commodity_market&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Crude oil and natural gas"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Chronology_of_world_oil_market_events" title="Chronology of world oil market events">Chronology of world oil market events</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Brent_Crude" title="Brent Crude">Brent Crude</a> serves as the world's leading crude oil benchmark, used to price more than 75% of globally traded oil cargoes due to its waterborne nature and broad market access.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>49<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Brent Crude, assessed daily by S&amp;P Global Commodity Insights as Dated Brent, reflects physical cargo loadings from the North Sea two weeks ahead.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>50<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In the United States, <a href="/wiki/West_Texas_Intermediate" title="West Texas Intermediate">West Texas Intermediate</a> (WTI) functions as the primary benchmark; this light, sweet crude grades as the underlying commodity for futures contracts on the New York Mercantile Exchange (NYMEX), part of CME Group.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>51<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> WTI, with lower sulfur content than Brent, trades at a premium to heavier grades such as Dubai or Oman.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>52<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Crude oil grades range from light to <a href="/wiki/Heavy_crude_oil" title="Heavy crude oil">heavy</a>, based on density and sulfur levels. As the first energy source to achieve widespread commodity trading status, oil prices remain sensitive to geopolitical events in producer nations, including <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>53<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> However, trading volumes on major exchanges reflect broader market participation beyond regional politics.
</p><p>Crude oil and refined products trade in standard contracts of 1,000 barrels (42,000 US gallons; 160,000 L).<sup id="cite&#95;ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>54<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> WTI futures appear under symbol CL on NYMEX and WBS on Intercontinental Exchange (ICE). Brent futures trade under BRN on ICE and BZ on CME. Refined products include Gulf Coast gasoline (symbol LR on NYMEX), reformulated gasoline blendstock for oxygen blending (RBOB; symbol RB on NYMEX), and propane (symbol PN on NYMEX, an ICE subsidiary since 2013).
</p><p>Natural gas trades in contracts of 10,000 million British thermal units (MMBtu).<sup id="cite&#95;ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>55<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Henry Hub natural gas futures use symbol NG on NYMEX. <a href="/wiki/Heating_oil" title="Heating oil">Heating oil</a> futures, symbol HO on NYMEX, represent ultra-low sulfur diesel equivalents in 42,000-gallon lots.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading4"><h4 id="Others">Others</h4><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commodity_market&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Others"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Purified_terephthalic_acid" class="mw-redirect" title="Purified terephthalic acid">Purified terephthalic acid</a> (PTA) is traded through ZCE in units of 5 tons with the trading symbol of TA. <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">Ethanol</a> is traded at <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Board_of_Trade" title="Chicago Board of Trade">CBOT</a> in units of 29,000 U.S. gal under trading symbols AC (Open Auction) and ZE (Electronic).
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Metals">Metals</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commodity_market&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Metals"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading4"><h4 id="Precious_metals">Precious metals</h4><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commodity_market&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Precious metals"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Precious_metal" title="Precious metal">Precious metals</a> currently traded on the commodity market include <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a>, <a href="/wiki/Platinum" title="Platinum">platinum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Palladium" title="Palladium">palladium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a> which are sold by the <a href="/wiki/Troy_ounce" class="mw-redirect" title="Troy ounce">troy ounce</a>. One of the main exchanges for these precious metals is <a href="/wiki/New_York_Mercantile_Exchange" title="New York Mercantile Exchange">COMEX</a>.
</p><p>According to the <a href="/wiki/World_Gold_Council" title="World Gold Council">World Gold Council</a>, investments in gold are the primary driver of industry growth. Gold prices are highly volatile, driven by large flows of speculative money.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-NYT13Apr2012&#95;59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT13Apr2012-59"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>56<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading4"><h4 id="Industrial_metals">Industrial metals</h4><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commodity_market&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Industrial metals"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Industrial metals are sold by the <a href="/wiki/Metric_ton" class="mw-redirect" title="Metric ton">metric ton</a> through the <a href="/wiki/London_Metal_Exchange" title="London Metal Exchange">London Metal Exchange</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_York_Mercantile_Exchange" title="New York Mercantile Exchange">New York Mercantile Exchange</a>. The London Metal Exchange trades include <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_alloy" title="Aluminium alloy">aluminium alloy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cobalt" title="Cobalt">cobalt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Molybdenum" title="Molybdenum">molybdenum</a>. In 2007, <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a> began trading on the London Metal Exchange.
</p><p>Iron ore has been the latest addition to industrial metal derivatives. Deutsche Bank first began offering iron ore swaps in 2008, other banks quickly followed. Since then the size of the market has more than doubled each year between 2008 and 2012.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>57<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Agriculture">Agriculture</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commodity_market&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Agriculture"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Hedge_(finance)#Agricultural_commodity_price_hedging" title="Hedge (finance)">Hedge (finance) §&#160;Agricultural commodity price hedging</a></div>
<p>Agricultural commodities include grains, food and fiber as well as livestock and meat, various regulatory bodies define agricultural products.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>58<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In 1900, corn acreage was double that of wheat in the United States. But from the 1930s through the 1970s soybean acreage surpassed corn. Early in the 1970s grain and soybean prices, which had been relatively stable, "soared to levels that were unimaginable at the time". There were a number of factors affecting prices including the "surge in crude oil prices caused by the Arab Oil Embargo in October 1973 (U.S. inflation reached 11% in 1975)".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-CRB&#95;62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRB-62"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>59<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>On 21 July 2010, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">United States Congress</a> passed the <a href="/wiki/Dodd%E2%80%93Frank_Wall_Street_Reform_and_Consumer_Protection_Act" title="Dodd–Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act">Dodd–Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act</a> with changes to the definition of agricultural commodity. The operational definition used by Dodd-Frank includes "[a]ll other commodities that are, or once were, or are derived from, living organisms, including plant, animal and aquatic life, which are generally fungible, within their respective classes, and are used primarily for human food, shelter, animal feed, or natural fiber". Three other categories were explained and listed.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-DoddFrankdefinition2010&#95;63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DoddFrankdefinition2010-63"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>60<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In February 2013, <a href="/wiki/Cornell_Law_School" title="Cornell Law School">Cornell Law School</a> included lumber, soybeans, oilseeds, livestock (live cattle and hogs), dairy products. Agricultural commodities can include lumber (timber and forests), grains excluding stored grain (wheat, oats, barley, rye, grain sorghum, cotton, flax, forage, tame hay, native grass), vegetables (potatoes, tomatoes, sweet corn, dry beans, dry peas, freezing and canning peas), fruit (citrus such as oranges, apples, grapes) corn, tobacco, rice, peanuts, sugar beets, sugar cane, sunflowers, raisins, nursery crops, nuts, soybean complex, aquacultural fish farm species such as finfish, mollusk, crustacean, aquatic invertebrate, amphibian, reptile, or plant life cultivated in aquatic plant farms.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-cornell&#95;64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cornell-64"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>61<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-reuters15april2013&#95;65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reuters15april2013-65"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>62<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Diamonds">Diamonds</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commodity_market&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Diamonds"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>As of 2012, diamond was not traded as a commodity. Institutional investors were repelled by campaign against "<a href="/wiki/Blood_diamonds" class="mw-redirect" title="Blood diamonds">blood diamonds</a>", the monopoly structure of the diamond market and the lack of uniform standards for diamond pricing. In 2012 the SEC reviewed a proposal to create the "first diamond-backed exchange-traded fund" that would trade online in units of one-carat diamonds with a storage vault and delivery point in Antwerp, home of the <a href="/wiki/World_Federation_of_Diamond_Bourses" title="World Federation of Diamond Bourses">Antwerp Diamond Bourse</a>. The exchange fund was backed by a company based in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> called <a href="/w/index.php?title=IndexIQ&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="IndexIQ (page does not exist)">IndexIQ</a>. IndexIQ had already introduced 14 exchange-traded funds since 2008.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-NYT13Apr2012&#95;59-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT13Apr2012-59"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>56<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>63<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>notes 4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>According to <a href="/wiki/Citigroup" title="Citigroup">Citigroup</a> analysts, the annual production of polished diamonds is about $18 billion. Like gold, diamonds are easily authenticated and durable. Diamond prices have been more stable than the metals, as the global diamond monopoly <a href="/wiki/De_Beers" title="De Beers">De Beers</a> once held almost 90% (by 2013 reduced to 40%) of the new diamond market.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-NYT13Apr2012&#95;59-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT13Apr2012-59"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>56<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Other_commodity_markets">Other commodity markets</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commodity_market&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Other commodity markets"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Rubber trades on the <a href="/wiki/Singapore_Commodity_Exchange" class="mw-redirect" title="Singapore Commodity Exchange">Singapore Commodity Exchange</a> in units of 1&#160;kg priced in U.S. cents. <a href="/wiki/Palm_oil" title="Palm oil">Palm oil</a> is traded on the <a href="/wiki/Malaysian_Ringgit" class="mw-redirect" title="Malaysian Ringgit">Malaysian Ringgit</a> (RM), <a href="/wiki/Bursa_Malaysia" title="Bursa Malaysia">Bursa Malaysia</a> in units of 1&#160;kg priced in U.S. cents. Wool is traded on the <a href="/wiki/AUD" class="mw-redirect" title="AUD">AUD</a> in units of 1&#160;kg. Polypropylene and Linear Low Density Polyethylene (LL) did trade on the London Metal Exchange in units of 1,000&#160;kg priced in USD but was dropped in 2011.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Impact_on_inflation">Impact on inflation</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commodity_market&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Impact on inflation"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel#Inflation_effects" title="Fossil fuel">Fossil fuels</a> and other commodities have been major drivers of inflationary periods, including the <a href="/wiki/2021-2022_inflation_spike" class="mw-redirect" title="2021-2022 inflation spike">2021-2022 inflation spike</a> exacerbated by the <a href="/wiki/Economic_impact_of_the_Russian_invasion_of_Ukraine" class="mw-redirect" title="Economic impact of the Russian invasion of Ukraine">Russian Invasion of Ukraine</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-cnn20220812&#95;68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cnn20220812-68"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>64<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-:1&#95;69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-69"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>65<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Gernot_Wagner" title="Gernot Wagner">Gernot Wagner</a> argues that commodites are undesirable energy sources because of inflationary periods that come with commodity prices.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-cnn20220812&#95;68-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cnn20220812-68"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>64<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>66<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In 2025, primary commodity prices fell 2.6% from March to August, with gains in precious metals offset by declines in energy and agriculture, contributing to stalled inflation near 3% in major economies.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>67<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Regulatory_bodies_and_policies">Regulatory bodies and policies</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commodity_market&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Regulatory bodies and policies"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="United_States">United States</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commodity_market&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: United States"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Commodity_Prices.webp" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Commodity_Prices.webp/250px-Commodity_Prices.webp.png" decoding="async" width="250" height="62" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Commodity_Prices.webp/500px-Commodity_Prices.webp.png 1.5x" data-file-width="5371" data-file-height="1335"></a><figcaption>Commodity Prices  <div class="legend"><span class="legend-line mw-no-invert" style="display: inline-block; vertical-align: middle; width: 1.67em; height: 0; border-style: none; border-top: 2px dotted black;border-top:#35DB3F solid 3px;">&#160;</span>&#160;<a href="/wiki/Soybean" title="Soybean">soybean</a></div> <div class="legend"><span class="legend-line mw-no-invert" style="display: inline-block; vertical-align: middle; width: 1.67em; height: 0; border-style: none; border-top: 2px dotted black;border-top:#DBC67D solid 3px;">&#160;</span>&#160;<a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a></div> <div class="legend"><span class="legend-line mw-no-invert" style="display: inline-block; vertical-align: middle; width: 1.67em; height: 0; border-style: none; border-top: 2px dotted black;border-top:yellow solid 3px;">&#160;</span>&#160;<a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">corn</a></div> <div class="legend"><span class="legend-line mw-no-invert" style="display: inline-block; vertical-align: middle; width: 1.67em; height: 0; border-style: none; border-top: 2px dotted black;border-top:#E8A02C solid 3px;">&#160;</span>&#160;<a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a></div> </figcaption></figure>
<p>In the United States, the principal regulator of commodity and futures markets is the <a href="/wiki/Commodity_Futures_Trading_Commission" title="Commodity Futures Trading Commission">Commodity Futures Trading Commission</a> (CFTC). The <a href="/wiki/National_Futures_Association" title="National Futures Association">National Futures Association</a> (NFA) was formed in 1976 and is the futures industry's self-regulatory organization. The NFA's first regulatory operations began in 1982 and fall under the <a href="/wiki/Commodity_Exchange_Act" title="Commodity Exchange Act">Commodity Exchange Act</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Commodity_Futures_Trading_Commission_Act_of_1974" title="Commodity Futures Trading Commission Act of 1974">Commodity Futures Trading Commission Act</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-NFA&#95;72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NFA-72"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>68<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Dodd–Frank was enacted in response to the 2008 financial crisis. It called for "strong measures to limit speculation in agricultural commodities" calling upon the CFTC to further limit positions and to regulate over-the-counter trades.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>69<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="European_Union">European Union</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Commodity_market&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: European Union"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Markets_in_Financial_Instruments_Directive" class="mw-redirect" title="Markets in Financial Instruments Directive">Markets in Financial Instruments Directive</a> (MiFID) is the cornerstone of the <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">European Commission</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Financial_Services_Action_Plan" title="Financial Services Action Plan">Financial Services Action Plan</a> that regulate operations of the EU financial service markets. It was reviewed in 2012 by the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">European Parliament</a> (EP) and the <a href="/wiki/Economic_and_Financial_Affairs_Council" title="Economic and Financial Affairs Council">Economic and Financial Affairs Council</a> (ECOFIN).<sup id="cite&#95;ref-SOMO26oct2012&#95;74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SOMO26oct2012-74"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>70<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The European Parliament adopted a revised version of Mifid II on 26 October 2012 which include "provisions for position limits on commodity derivatives", aimed at "preventing market abuse" and supporting "orderly pricing and settlement conditions".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-MifidII&#95;75-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MifidII-75"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>71<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/European_Securities_and_Markets_Authority" title="European Securities and Markets Authority">European Securities and Markets Authority</a> (Esma), based in Paris and formed in 2011, is an "EU-wide financial markets watchdog". Esma sets position limits on commodity derivatives as described in Mifid II.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-MifidII&#95;75-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MifidII-75"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>71<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The EP voted in favor of stronger regulation of commodity derivative markets in September 2012 to "end abusive speculation in commodity markets" that were "driving global food prices increases and price volatility". In July 2012, "food prices globally soared by 10 percent" (<a href="/wiki/World_Bank" class="mw-redirect" title="World Bank">World Bank</a> 2012). Senior British MEP Arlene McCarthy called for "putting a brake on excessive <a href="/wiki/Food_speculation" title="Food speculation">food speculation</a> and speculating giants profiting from hunger" ending immoral practices that "only serve the interests of profiteers".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-EU&#95;76-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EU-76"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>72<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In March 2012, EP Member Markus Ferber suggested amendments to the European Commission's proposals, intended to strengthen restrictions on <a href="/wiki/High-frequency_trading" title="High-frequency trading">high-frequency trading</a> and commodity price manipulation.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>73<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
