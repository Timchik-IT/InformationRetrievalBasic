					<div id="mw-content-text" class="mw-body-content"><div class="mw-subjectpageheader">
</div><div class="mw-content-ltr mw-parser-output" lang="en" dir="ltr"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Field of electrical engineering</div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">"Signal theory" redirects here; not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Signalling_theory" title="Signalling theory">Signalling theory</a> or <a href="/wiki/Signalling_(economics)" title="Signalling (economics)">Signalling (economics)</a>.</div>
<figure typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Signal_processing_system.png" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Signal_processing_system.png/500px-Signal_processing_system.png" decoding="async" width="400" height="129" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Signal_processing_system.png/960px-Signal_processing_system.png 1.5x" data-file-width="1204" data-file-height="387"></a><figcaption>Signal transmission using electronic signal processing. <a href="/wiki/Transducer" title="Transducer">Transducers</a> convert signals from other physical <a href="/wiki/Waveform" title="Waveform">waveforms</a> to electric <a href="/wiki/Electric_current" title="Electric current">current</a> or <a href="/wiki/Voltage" title="Voltage">voltage</a> waveforms, which then are processed, transmitted as <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_wave" class="mw-redirect" title="Electromagnetic wave">electromagnetic waves</a>, received and converted by another transducer to final form.</figcaption></figure>
<div class="thumb tmulti tright"><div class="thumbinner multiimageinner" style="width:408px;max-width:408px"><div class="trow"><div class="tsingle" style="width:202px;max-width:202px"><div class="thumbimage"><span typeof="mw:File"><span><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Cosine_Series_Plus_Noise.png/250px-Cosine_Series_Plus_Noise.png" decoding="async" width="200" height="150" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Cosine_Series_Plus_Noise.png/330px-Cosine_Series_Plus_Noise.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Cosine_Series_Plus_Noise.png/500px-Cosine_Series_Plus_Noise.png 2x" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="900"></span></span></div></div><div class="tsingle" style="width:202px;max-width:202px"><div class="thumbimage"><span typeof="mw:File"><span><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Cosine_Series_Plus_Noise_TFM.png/250px-Cosine_Series_Plus_Noise_TFM.png" decoding="async" width="200" height="150" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Cosine_Series_Plus_Noise_TFM.png/330px-Cosine_Series_Plus_Noise_TFM.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Cosine_Series_Plus_Noise_TFM.png/500px-Cosine_Series_Plus_Noise_TFM.png 2x" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="900"></span></span></div></div></div><div class="trow" style="display:flex"><div class="thumbcaption">The signal on the left looks like noise, but the signal processing technique known as <a href="/wiki/Spectral_density_estimation" title="Spectral density estimation">spectral density estimation</a> (right) shows that it contains five well-defined frequency components.</div></div></div></div>
<p><b>Signal processing</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Electrical_engineering" title="Electrical engineering">electrical engineering</a> subfield that focuses on analyzing, modifying and synthesizing <i><a href="/wiki/Signal" title="Signal">signals</a></i>, such as <a href="/wiki/Audio_signal_processing" title="Audio signal processing">sound</a>, <a href="/wiki/Image_processing" class="mw-redirect" title="Image processing">images</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scalar_potential" title="Scalar potential">potential fields</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seismic_tomography" title="Seismic tomography">seismic signals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Altimeter" title="Altimeter">altimetry processing</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Scientific_measurements" class="mw-redirect" title="Scientific measurements">scientific measurements</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Signal processing techniques are used to optimize transmissions, <a href="/wiki/Digital_storage" class="mw-redirect" title="Digital storage">digital storage</a> efficiency, correcting distorted signals, improve <a href="/wiki/Subjective_video_quality" title="Subjective video quality">subjective video quality</a>, and to detect or pinpoint components of interest in a measured signal.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="History">History</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Signal_processing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Alan_V._Oppenheim" title="Alan V. Oppenheim">Alan V. Oppenheim</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ronald_W._Schafer" title="Ronald W. Schafer">Ronald W. Schafer</a>, the principles of signal processing can be found in the classical <a href="/wiki/Numerical_analysis" title="Numerical analysis">numerical analysis</a> techniques of the 17th century.  They further state that the digital refinement of these techniques can be found in the digital <a href="/wiki/Control_system" title="Control system">control systems</a> of the 1940s and 1950s.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In 1948, <a href="/wiki/Claude_Shannon" title="Claude Shannon">Claude Shannon</a> wrote the influential paper "<a href="/wiki/A_Mathematical_Theory_of_Communication" title="A Mathematical Theory of Communication">A Mathematical Theory of Communication</a>" which was published in the <i><a href="/wiki/Bell_System_Technical_Journal" class="mw-redirect" title="Bell System Technical Journal">Bell System Technical Journal</a></i>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>  The paper laid the groundwork for later development of information communication systems and the processing of signals for transmission.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-fifty&#95;5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fifty-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Signal processing matured and flourished in the 1960s and 1970s, and <a href="/wiki/Digital_signal_processing" title="Digital signal processing">digital signal processing</a> became widely used with specialized <a href="/wiki/Digital_signal_processor" title="Digital signal processor">digital signal processor</a> chips in the 1980s.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-fifty&#95;5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fifty-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Analog">Analog</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Signal_processing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Analog"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Analog_signal_processing" title="Analog signal processing">Analog signal processing</a></div>
<p>Analog signal processing is for signals that have not been digitized, as in most 20th-century <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">radio</a>, telephone, and television systems. This involves linear electronic circuits as well as nonlinear ones. The former are, for instance, <a href="/wiki/Passive_filter" class="mw-redirect" title="Passive filter">passive filters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Active_filter" title="Active filter">active filters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electronic_mixer" title="Electronic mixer">additive mixers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Integrator" title="Integrator">integrators</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Analog_delay_line" title="Analog delay line">delay lines</a>. Nonlinear circuits include <a href="/wiki/Compandor" class="mw-redirect" title="Compandor">compandors</a>, multipliers (<a href="/wiki/Frequency_mixer" title="Frequency mixer">frequency mixers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Voltage-controlled_amplifier" class="mw-redirect" title="Voltage-controlled amplifier">voltage-controlled amplifiers</a>), <a href="/wiki/Voltage-controlled_filter" title="Voltage-controlled filter">voltage-controlled filters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Voltage-controlled_oscillator" title="Voltage-controlled oscillator">voltage-controlled oscillators</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Phase-locked_loop" title="Phase-locked loop">phase-locked loops</a>.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Continuous_time">Continuous time</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Signal_processing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Continuous time"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Continuous-time_signal" class="mw-redirect" title="Continuous-time signal">Continuous-time signal</a> processing is for signals that vary with the change of continuous domain (without considering some individual interrupted points).
</p><p>The methods of signal processing include <a href="/wiki/Time_domain" title="Time domain">time domain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frequency_domain" title="Frequency domain">frequency domain</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Complex_frequency" class="mw-redirect" title="Complex frequency">complex frequency domain</a>. This technology mainly discusses the modeling of a <a href="/wiki/Linear_time-invariant" class="mw-redirect" title="Linear time-invariant">linear time-invariant</a> continuous system, integral of the system's zero-state response, setting up system function and the continuous time filtering of deterministic signals. <div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Discrete_time">Discrete time</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Signal_processing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Discrete time"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Discrete-time_signal" class="mw-redirect" title="Discrete-time signal">Discrete-time signal</a> processing is for sampled signals, defined only at discrete points in time, and as such are quantized in time, but not in magnitude.
</p><p><i>Analog discrete-time signal processing</i> is a technology based on electronic devices such as <a href="/wiki/Sample_and_hold" title="Sample and hold">sample and hold</a> circuits, analog time-division <a href="/wiki/Multiplexer" title="Multiplexer">multiplexers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Analog_delay_line" title="Analog delay line">analog delay lines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Analog_feedback_shift_register" title="Analog feedback shift register">analog feedback shift registers</a>. This technology was a predecessor of digital signal processing (see below), and is still used in advanced processing of gigahertz signals.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The concept of discrete-time signal processing also refers to a theoretical discipline that establishes a mathematical basis for digital signal processing, without taking <a href="/wiki/Quantization_error" class="mw-redirect" title="Quantization error">quantization error</a> into consideration.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Digital">Digital</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Signal_processing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Digital"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Digital_signal_processing" title="Digital signal processing">Digital signal processing</a></div>
<p>Digital signal processing is the processing of digitized discrete-time sampled signals. Processing is done by general-purpose <a href="/wiki/Computer" title="Computer">computers</a> or by digital circuits such as <a href="/wiki/ASIC" class="mw-redirect" title="ASIC">ASICs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Field-programmable_gate_array" title="Field-programmable gate array">field-programmable gate arrays</a> or specialized <a href="/wiki/Digital_signal_processor" title="Digital signal processor">digital signal processors</a>. Typical arithmetical operations include <a href="/wiki/Fixed-point_arithmetic" title="Fixed-point arithmetic">fixed-point</a> and <a href="/wiki/Floating-point" class="mw-redirect" title="Floating-point">floating-point</a>, real-valued and complex-valued, multiplication and addition. Other typical operations supported by the hardware are <a href="/wiki/Circular_buffer" title="Circular buffer">circular buffers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lookup_table" title="Lookup table">lookup tables</a>. Examples of algorithms are the <a href="/wiki/Fast_Fourier_transform" title="Fast Fourier transform">fast Fourier transform</a> (FFT), <a href="/wiki/Finite_impulse_response" title="Finite impulse response">finite impulse response</a> (FIR) filter, <a href="/wiki/Infinite_impulse_response" title="Infinite impulse response">Infinite impulse response</a> (IIR) filter, and <a href="/wiki/Adaptive_filter" title="Adaptive filter">adaptive filters</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Wiener_filter" title="Wiener filter">Wiener</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kalman_filter" title="Kalman filter">Kalman filters</a>.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Nonlinear">Nonlinear</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Signal_processing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Nonlinear"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Nonlinear signal processing involves the analysis and processing of signals produced from <a href="/wiki/Nonlinear_system" title="Nonlinear system">nonlinear systems</a> and can be in the time, <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">frequency</a>, or spatiotemporal domains.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Billings&#95;8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Billings-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-VSA&#95;9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VSA-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Nonlinear systems can produce highly complex behaviors including <a href="/wiki/Bifurcation_theory" title="Bifurcation theory">bifurcations</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chaos_theory" title="Chaos theory">chaos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harmonics" class="mw-redirect" title="Harmonics">harmonics</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Subharmonics" class="mw-redirect" title="Subharmonics">subharmonics</a> which cannot be produced or analyzed using linear methods. 
</p><p>Polynomial signal processing is a type of non-linear signal processing, where <a href="/wiki/Polynomial" title="Polynomial">polynomial</a> systems may be interpreted as conceptually straightforward extensions of linear systems to the nonlinear case.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Statistical">Statistical</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Signal_processing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Statistical"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p><b>Statistical signal processing</b> is an approach which treats signals as <a href="/wiki/Stochastic_process" title="Stochastic process">stochastic processes</a>, utilizing their <a href="/wiki/Statistical" class="mw-redirect" title="Statistical">statistical</a> properties to perform signal processing tasks.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Scharf&#95;11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scharf-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Statistical techniques are widely used in signal processing applications. For example, one can model the <a href="/wiki/Probability_distribution" title="Probability distribution">probability distribution</a> of noise incurred when photographing an image, and construct techniques based on this model to <a href="/wiki/Noise_reduction" title="Noise reduction">reduce the noise</a> in the resulting image.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Graph">Graph</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Signal_processing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Graph"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p><b>Graph signal processing</b> generalizes signal processing tasks to signals living on non-Euclidean domains whose structure can be captured by a weighted graph.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Ortega&#95;12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ortega-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Graph signal processing presents several key points such as sampling signal techniques,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Tanaka&#95;13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tanaka-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> recovery techniques  <sup id="cite&#95;ref-Fascista&#95;14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fascista-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and time-varying techiques.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Giraldo&#95;15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Giraldo-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Graph signal processing has been applied with success in the field of image processing, computer vision <sup id="cite&#95;ref-Giraldo1&#95;16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Giraldo1-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> 
<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Giraldo2&#95;17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Giraldo2-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> 
<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Giraldo3&#95;18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Giraldo3-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and sound anomaly detection.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Bouwmans1&#95;19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bouwmans1-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
