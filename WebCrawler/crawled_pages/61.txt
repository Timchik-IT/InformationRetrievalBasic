					<div id="mw-content-text" class="mw-body-content"><div class="mw-subjectpageheader">
</div><div class="mw-content-ltr mw-parser-output" lang="en" dir="ltr"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Contractual transaction on a decentralized platform</div>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1320445320">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}@media print{body.ns-0 .mw-parser-output .hatnote{display:none!important}}</style><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">This article is about contractual transactions on a decentralized platform. For smart legal contracts, see <a href="/wiki/Smart_legal_contract" class="mw-redirect" title="Smart legal contract">Smart legal contract</a>.</div>
<p>
A <b>smart contract</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Computer_program" title="Computer program">computer program</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Transaction_Protocol_Data_Unit" title="Transaction Protocol Data Unit">transaction protocol</a> that is intended to automatically execute, control or document events and actions according to the terms of a <a href="/wiki/Contract" title="Contract">contract</a> or an agreement.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-infobus&#95;1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-infobus-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-conbook&#95;2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-conbook-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-savel&#95;3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-savel-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-tapscott2016&#95;4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tapscott2016-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The objectives of smart contracts are the reduction of need for trusted intermediators, arbitration costs, and fraud losses, as well as the reduction of malicious and accidental exceptions.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-conbook&#95;2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-conbook-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Smart contracts are commonly associated with <a href="/wiki/Cryptocurrency" title="Cryptocurrency">cryptocurrencies</a>, and the smart contracts introduced by <a href="/wiki/Ethereum" title="Ethereum">Ethereum</a> are generally considered a fundamental building block for <a href="/wiki/Decentralized_finance" title="Decentralized finance">decentralized finance</a> (DeFi) and <a href="/wiki/Non-fungible_token" title="Non-fungible token">non-fungible token</a> (NFT) applications.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The original <a href="/wiki/Ethereum" title="Ethereum">Ethereum</a> <a href="/wiki/White_paper" title="White paper">white paper</a> by <a href="/wiki/Vitalik_Buterin" title="Vitalik Buterin">Vitalik Buterin</a> in 2014<sup id="cite&#95;ref-buterin&#95;7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-buterin-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> describes the <a href="/wiki/Bitcoin_protocol" title="Bitcoin protocol">Bitcoin protocol</a> as a weak version of the smart contract concept as originally defined by <a href="/wiki/Nick_Szabo" title="Nick Szabo">Nick Szabo</a>, and proposed a stronger version based on the <a href="/wiki/Solidity" title="Solidity">Solidity</a> language, which is <a href="/wiki/Turing_complete" class="mw-redirect" title="Turing complete">Turing complete</a>. Since then, various cryptocurrencies have supported <a href="/wiki/Programming_language" title="Programming language">programming languages</a> which allow for more advanced smart contracts between untrusted parties.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>A smart contract should not be confused with a <a href="/wiki/Smart_legal_contract" class="mw-redirect" title="Smart legal contract">smart legal contract</a>, which is a traditional, natural-language, legally binding agreement that has selected terms expressed and implemented in machine-readable code.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-erpl201812&#95;9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-erpl201812-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-jit202008&#95;10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jit202008-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-jit202009&#95;11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jit202009-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Etymology">Etymology</h2></div>
<p>By 1996, <a href="/wiki/Nick_Szabo" title="Nick Szabo">Nick Szabo</a> was using the term "smart contract" to refer to contracts which would be enforced by physical property (such as hardware or software) instead of by law. Szabo described <a href="/wiki/Vending_machine" title="Vending machine">vending machines</a> as an example of this concept.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In 1998, the term was used to describe objects in <a href="/wiki/Digital_rights_management" title="Digital rights management">rights management service layer</a> of the system <i>The Stanford Infobus</i>, which was a part of <a href="/wiki/Stanford_Digital_Library_Project" title="Stanford Digital Library Project">Stanford Digital Library Project</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-infobus&#95;1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-infobus-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Legal_status_of_smart_contracts">Legal status of smart contracts</h2></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1320445320" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Regulation_of_algorithms" title="Regulation of algorithms">Regulation of algorithms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Distributed_ledger_technology_law" title="Distributed ledger technology law">distributed ledger technology law</a></div>
<p>A <i>smart contract</i> does not typically constitute a valid binding agreement at law.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-jcl201912&#95;14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jcl201912-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Proposals exist to regulate smart contracts.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-erpl201812&#95;9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-erpl201812-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-jit202008&#95;10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jit202008-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-jit202009&#95;11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jit202009-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Smart contracts are not legal agreements, but instead transactions which are executed automatically by a <a href="/wiki/Computer_program" title="Computer program">computer program</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Protocol_(computer_science)" class="mw-redirect" title="Protocol (computer science)">transaction protocol</a>,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-jcl201912&#95;14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jcl201912-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> such as technological means for the automation of payment obligations<sup id="cite&#95;ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> such as by transferring cryptocurrencies or other tokens. Some scholars have argued that the imperative or declarative nature of programming languages would impact the legal validity of smart contracts.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-ail2018issue4&#95;16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ail2018issue4-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In some jurisdictions, legal scholars have examined how the rigidity of smart contracts interacts with traditional doctrines such as contractual unforeseeability. For instance, Colombian legal scholarship has proposed adapting the theory of supervening onerousness (teoría de la imprevisión) to account for the high economic and systemic costs of reversing smart contract effects through judicial intervention, emphasizing the need to internalize these costs and develop new procedural mechanisms for digital environments.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Since the 2015 launch of the <a href="/wiki/Ethereum" title="Ethereum">Ethereum</a> <a href="/wiki/Blockchain_(database)" class="mw-redirect" title="Blockchain (database)">blockchain</a>, the term "smart contract" has been applied to general purpose computation that takes place on a blockchain. The US National Institute of Standards and Technology describes a "smart contract" as a "collection of code and data (sometimes referred to as functions and state) that is deployed using cryptographically signed transactions on the blockchain network".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In this interpretation a smart contract is any kind of computer program which uses a blockchain. A smart contract also can be regarded as a secured <a href="/wiki/Stored_procedure" title="Stored procedure">stored procedure</a>, as its execution and codified effects (like the transfer of tokens between parties) cannot be manipulated without modifying the blockchain itself. In this interpretation, the execution of contracts is controlled and audited by the platform, not by arbitrary server-side programs connecting to the platform.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In 2018, a <a href="/wiki/US_Senate" class="mw-redirect" title="US Senate">US Senate</a> report said:  "While smart contracts might sound new, the concept is rooted in basic contract law. Usually, the judicial system adjudicates contractual disputes and enforces terms, but it is also common to have another arbitration method, especially for international transactions. With smart contracts, a program enforces the contract built into the code."<sup id="cite&#95;ref-usSenate201803&#95;21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-usSenate201803-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> States in the US which have passed legislation on the use of smart contracts include Arizona,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Iowa,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>23<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Nevada,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Tennessee,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>25<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and Wyoming.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>26<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In April 2021, the UK Jurisdiction Taskforce (UKJT) published the Digital Dispute Resolution Rules (the Digital DR Rules), which were intended to enable the rapid resolution of blockchain and crypto legal disputes in Britain.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>27<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In 2021, the Law Commission of England and Wales advised that smart legal contracts are capable of being recognized and enforced under existing English law.<a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="https://lawcom.gov.uk/project/smart-contracts/">[1]</a>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Workings">Workings</h2></div>
<p>Similar to a transfer of value on a blockchain, deployment of a smart contract on a blockchain occurs by sending a transaction from a <a href="/wiki/Cryptocurrency_wallet" title="Cryptocurrency wallet">wallet</a> for the blockchain.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-SoloroKannaHoover&#95;28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SoloroKannaHoover-28"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>28<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>  The transaction includes the compiled code for the smart contract as well as a special receiver address.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-SoloroKannaHoover&#95;28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SoloroKannaHoover-28"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>28<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> That transaction must then be included in a block that is added to the blockchain, at which point the smart contract's code will execute to establish the initial state of the smart contract.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-SoloroKannaHoover&#95;28-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SoloroKannaHoover-28"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>28<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_fault_tolerance" class="mw-redirect" title="Byzantine fault tolerance">Byzantine fault-tolerant</a> algorithms secure the smart contract in a decentralized way from attempts to tamper with it. Once a smart contract is deployed, it cannot be updated.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-zilliqa&#95;29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zilliqa-29"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>29<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Smart contracts on a blockchain can store arbitrary state and execute arbitrary computations. End clients interact with a smart contract through transactions. Such transactions with a smart contract can invoke other smart contracts. These transactions might result in changing the state and sending coins from one smart contract to another or from one account to another.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-zilliqa&#95;29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zilliqa-29"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>29<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The most popular blockchain for running smart contracts is <a href="/wiki/Ethereum" title="Ethereum">Ethereum</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>30<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> On Ethereum, <a href="/wiki/Smart_contracts" class="mw-redirect" title="Smart contracts">smart contracts</a> are typically written in a <a href="/wiki/Turing-completeness" class="mw-redirect" title="Turing-completeness">Turing-complete</a> programming language called <a href="/wiki/Solidity" title="Solidity">Solidity</a>,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>31<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and compiled into low-level <a href="/wiki/Bytecode" title="Bytecode">bytecode</a> to be executed by the <a href="/wiki/Ethereum#Virtual_machine" title="Ethereum">Ethereum Virtual Machine</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-vuln-exploit&#95;32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vuln-exploit-32"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>32<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Due to the <a href="/wiki/Halting_problem" title="Halting problem">halting problem</a> and other security problems, Turing-completeness is considered to be a risk and is deliberately avoided by languages like <a href="/wiki/Ethereum#Contract_source_code" title="Ethereum">Vyper</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-harz&#95;33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harz-33"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>33<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-overview&#95;34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-overview-34"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>34<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Some of the other smart contract programming languages missing Turing-completeness are Simplicity, Scilla, Ivy and Bitcoin Script.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-overview&#95;34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-overview-34"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>34<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Some newer platforms have explored "asset-oriented" domain-specific languages (such as Scrypto) that treat digital assets as native data types within the language environment to enforce finiteness and safety rules at the compiler level.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-CryptoOasis24&#95;35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CryptoOasis24-35"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>35<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> However, measurements in 2020 using <a href="/wiki/Regular_expression" title="Regular expression">regular expressions</a> showed that only 35.3% of 53,757 Ethereum smart contracts at that time included recursions and loops — constructs connected to the halting problem.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-needturing&#95;36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-needturing-36"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>36<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Several languages are designed to enable <a href="/wiki/Formal_verification" title="Formal verification">formal verification</a>: Bamboo, IELE, Simplicity, Michelson (can be verified with <a href="/wiki/Rocq" title="Rocq">Rocq</a>),<sup id="cite&#95;ref-overview&#95;34-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-overview-34"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>34<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Liquidity (compiles to Michelson), Scilla, DAML and Pact.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-harz&#95;33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harz-33"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>33<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Applications">Applications</h2></div>
<p>In 1998, Szabo proposed that smart contract infrastructure can be implemented by replicated asset registries and contract execution using <a href="/wiki/Merkle_trees" class="mw-redirect" title="Merkle trees">cryptographic hash chains</a> and <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_fault_tolerance" class="mw-redirect" title="Byzantine fault tolerance">Byzantine fault-tolerant</a> replication.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>48<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Askemos implemented this approach in 2002<sup id="cite&#95;ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>49<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>50<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> using <a href="/wiki/Scheme_(programming_language)" title="Scheme (programming language)">Scheme</a> (later adding <a href="/wiki/SQLite" title="SQLite">SQLite</a><sup id="cite&#95;ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>51<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>52<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>) as the contract script language.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>53<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>One proposal for using Bitcoin for replicated asset registration and contract execution is called "<a href="/wiki/Colored_Coins" title="Colored Coins">colored coins</a>".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>54<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Replicated titles for potentially arbitrary forms of property, along with replicated contract execution, are implemented in different projects.
</p><p>As of 2015<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Smart_contract&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>, <a href="/wiki/UBS" title="UBS">UBS</a> was experimenting with "smart bonds" that use the <a href="/wiki/Bitcoin" title="Bitcoin">bitcoin</a> <a href="/wiki/Blockchain_(database)" class="mw-redirect" title="Blockchain (database)">blockchain</a><sup id="cite&#95;ref-newsweek20150912&#95;55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newsweek20150912-55"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>55<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> in which payment streams could hypothetically be fully automated, creating a self-paying instrument.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-IFRasia20150611&#95;56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IFRasia20150611-56"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>56<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Inheritance" title="Inheritance">Inheritance</a> wishes could hypothetically be implemented automatically upon registration of a <a href="/wiki/Death_certificate" title="Death certificate">death certificate</a> by means of smart contracts.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (January 2022)">according to whom?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>57<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>58<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Birth_certificate" title="Birth certificate">Birth certificates</a> can also work together with smart contracts.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>59<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>60<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Chris Snook of <i><a href="/wiki/Inc.com" class="mw-redirect" title="Inc.com">Inc.com</a></i> suggests smart contracts could also be used to handle <a href="/wiki/Real_estate" title="Real estate">real estate</a> transactions and could be used in the field of <a href="/wiki/Title_search" title="Title search">title records</a> and in the <a href="/wiki/Public_records" title="Public records">public register</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Snook&#95;2017&#95;61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snook_2017-61"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>61<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>62<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>63<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>64<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>65<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Seth Oranburg and Liya Palagashvili argue that smart contracts could also be used in <a href="/wiki/Employment_contract" title="Employment contract">employment contracts</a>, especially temporary employment contracts, which according to them would benefit the employer.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Oranburg&#95;Palagashvili&#95;2018&#95;66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oranburg_Palagashvili_2018-66"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>66<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>67<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Security_issues">Security issues</h2></div>
<p>The transactions data from a blockchain-based smart contract is visible to all users in the blockchain. The data provides cryptographic view of the transactions, however, this leads to a situation where bugs, including security holes, are visible to all yet may not be quickly fixed.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Peck20160528&#95;68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Peck20160528-68"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>68<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Such an attack, difficult to fix quickly, was successfully executed on <a href="/wiki/The_DAO_(organization)" class="mw-redirect" title="The DAO (organization)">The DAO</a> in June 2016, draining approximately <span style="white-space: nowrap">US$50</span><span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>million worth of <a href="/wiki/Ethereum#Ether" title="Ethereum">Ether</a> at the time, while developers attempted to come to a solution that would gain consensus.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:0&#95;69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-69"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>69<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>  The DAO program had a time delay in place before the hacker could remove the funds; a hard fork of the Ethereum software was done to claw back the funds from the attacker before the time limit expired.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>70<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Other high-profile attacks include the <a href="/wiki/Cryptocurrency_and_crime#Wallets" title="Cryptocurrency and crime">Parity multisignature wallet attacks</a>, and an integer underflow/overflow attack (2018), totaling over <span style="white-space: nowrap">US$184</span><span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>million.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>71<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Issues in Ethereum smart contracts, in particular, include ambiguities and easy-but-insecure constructs in its contract language Solidity, compiler bugs, Ethereum Virtual Machine bugs, attacks on the blockchain network, the immutability of bugs and that there is no central source documenting known vulnerabilities, attacks and problematic constructs.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:1&#95;37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-37"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>37<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Difference_from_smart_legal_contracts">Difference from smart legal contracts</h2></div>
<p>Smart legal contracts are distinct from smart contracts. As mentioned above, a smart contract is not necessarily legally enforceable as a contract. On the other hand, a smart legal contract has all the elements of a legally enforceable contract in the jurisdiction in which it can be enforced and it can be enforced by a court or tribunal. Therefore, while every smart legal contract will contain some elements of a smart contract, not every smart contract will be a smart legal contract.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>72<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>There is no formal definition of a smart legal contract in the legal industry.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>73<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Ricardian_contract" title="Ricardian contract">Ricardian contract</a> is a type of smart legal contract.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2023)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
