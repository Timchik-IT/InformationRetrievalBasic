</div><div class="mw-content-ltr mw-parser-output" lang="en" dir="ltr"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Forecast_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Forecast (disambiguation)">Forecast</a>.</div>
<div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Making predictions based on available data</div>
<p><b>Forecasting</b> is the process of making predictions based on <a href="/wiki/Past" title="Past">past</a> and present data. These forecasts can later be compared with actual outcomes. For example, a company might <a href="/wiki/Estimation" title="Estimation">estimate</a> their revenue in the next year, then compare it against the actual results creating a variance actual analysis. <a href="/wiki/Prediction" title="Prediction">Prediction</a> is a similar but more general term. Forecasting might refer to specific formal statistical methods employing <a href="/wiki/Time_series" title="Time series">time series</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cross-sectional_data" title="Cross-sectional data">cross-sectional</a> or <a href="/wiki/Longitudinal_study" title="Longitudinal study">longitudinal</a> data, or alternatively to less formal judgmental methods or the process of prediction and assessment of its accuracy. Usage can vary between areas of application: for example, in <a href="/wiki/Hydrology" title="Hydrology">hydrology</a> the terms "forecast" and "forecasting" are sometimes reserved for estimates of values at certain specific <a href="/wiki/Future" title="Future">future</a> times, while the term "prediction" is used for more general estimates, such as the number of times floods will occur over a long period.
<sup id="cite&#95;ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p><br>
<a href="/wiki/Risk" title="Risk">Risk</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uncertainty" title="Uncertainty">uncertainty</a> are central to forecasting and prediction; it is generally considered a good practice to indicate the degree of uncertainty attaching to forecasts. In any case, the data must be up to date in order for the forecast to be as accurate as possible. In some cases the data used to predict the variable of interest is itself forecast.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> A forecast is not to be confused with a Budget; budgets are more specific, fixed-term financial plans used for resource allocation and control, while forecasts provide estimates of future financial performance, allowing for flexibility and adaptability to changing circumstances.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>  Both tools are valuable in financial planning and decision-making, but they serve different functions.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Applications">Applications</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Applications"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Forecasting has applications in a wide range of fields where estimates of future conditions are useful. Depending on the field, accuracy varies significantly. If the factors that relate to what is being forecast are known and well understood and there is a significant amount of data that can be used, it is likely the final value will be close to the forecast. If this is not the case or if the actual outcome is affected by the forecasts, the reliability of the forecasts can be significantly lower.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-https&#58;//www.otexts.org/fpp/1/1&#95;4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-https://www.otexts.org/fpp/1/1-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> 
</p><p>Climate change and increasing energy prices have led to the use of <a href="/wiki/Egain_Forecasting" class="mw-redirect" title="Egain Forecasting">Egain Forecasting</a> for buildings. This attempts to reduce the energy needed to heat the building, thus reducing the emission of <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a>. Forecasting is used in <a href="/wiki/Customer_demand_planning" title="Customer demand planning">customer demand planning</a> in everyday business for manufacturing and distribution companies.
</p><p>While the veracity of predictions for actual stock returns are disputed through reference to the <a href="/wiki/Efficient-market_hypothesis" title="Efficient-market hypothesis">efficient-market hypothesis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Economic_forecasting" title="Economic forecasting">forecasting of broad economic trends</a> is common. Such analysis is provided by both non-profit groups as well as by for-profit private institutions.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Forecasting <a href="/wiki/Exchange_rate" title="Exchange rate">foreign exchange</a> movements is typically achieved through a combination of historical and current data (summarized in charts) and <a href="/wiki/Fundamental_analysis" title="Fundamental analysis">fundamental analysis</a>. An essential difference between chart analysis and fundamental economic analysis is that chartists study only the price action of a market, whereas fundamentalists attempt to look to the reasons behind the action.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Financial institutions assimilate the evidence provided by their fundamental and chartist researchers into one note to provide a final projection on the currency in question.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-InstutionalResearch&#95;6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-InstutionalResearch-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Forecasting has also been used to predict the development of conflict situations.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Forecasters perform research that uses empirical results to gauge the effectiveness of certain forecasting models.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-faq&#95;8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-faq-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> However research has shown that there is little difference between the accuracy of the forecasts of experts knowledgeable in the conflict situation and those by individuals who knew much less.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Similarly, experts in some studies argue that role thinking — standing in other people's shoes to forecast their decisions — does not contribute to the accuracy of the forecast.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> 
</p><p>An important, albeit often ignored aspect of forecasting, is the relationship it holds with <a href="/wiki/Planning" title="Planning">planning</a>. Forecasting can be described as predicting what the future <i>will</i> look like, whereas planning predicts what the future <i>should</i> look like.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-faq&#95;8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-faq-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> There is no single right forecasting method to use. Selection of a method should be based on your objectives and your conditions (data etc.).<sup id="cite&#95;ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> A good way to find a method is by visiting a selection tree. An example of a selection tree can be found here.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Forecasting has application in many situations:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Supply_chain_management" title="Supply chain management">Supply chain management</a> and <a href="/wiki/Customer_demand_planning" title="Customer demand planning">customer demand planning</a> — Forecasting can be used in supply chain management to ensure that the right product is at the right place at the right time. Accurate forecasting will help retailers reduce excess inventory and thus increase <a href="/wiki/Profit_margin" title="Profit margin">profit margin</a>. Accurate forecasting will also help them meet consumer demand. The discipline of demand planning, also sometimes referred to as supply chain forecasting, embraces both statistical forecasting and a consensus process. Studies have shown that extrapolations are the least accurate, while company earnings forecasts are the most reliable.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (December 2013)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Economic_forecasting" title="Economic forecasting">Economic forecasting</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Earthquake_prediction" title="Earthquake prediction">Earthquake prediction</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Egain_forecasting" title="Egain forecasting">Egain forecasting</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Energy_forecasting" title="Energy forecasting">Energy forecasting</a> for renewable power integration</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Finance" title="Finance">Finance</a> against risk of <a href="/wiki/Default_(finance)" title="Default (finance)">default</a> via <a href="/wiki/Credit_rating" title="Credit rating">credit ratings</a> and <a href="/wiki/Credit_score" title="Credit score">credit scores</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Land_use_forecasting" class="mw-redirect" title="Land use forecasting">Land use forecasting</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/PECOTA" title="PECOTA">Player and team performance in sports</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Political_forecasting" title="Political forecasting">Political forecasting</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Product_forecasting" title="Product forecasting">Product forecasting</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sales_forecasting" class="mw-redirect" title="Sales forecasting">Sales forecasting</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Technology_forecasting" title="Technology forecasting">Technology forecasting</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Telecommunications_forecasting" title="Telecommunications forecasting">Telecommunications forecasting</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Transport_planning" class="mw-redirect" title="Transport planning">Transport planning</a> and <a href="/wiki/Transportation_forecasting" title="Transportation forecasting">forecasting</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Weather_forecasting" title="Weather forecasting">Weather forecasting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flood_forecasting" title="Flood forecasting">flood forecasting</a> and <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorology</a></li></ul>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Forecasting_as_training,_betting_and_futarchy"><span id="Forecasting_as_training.2C_betting_and_futarchy"></span>Forecasting as training, betting and futarchy</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Forecasting as training, betting and futarchy"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>In several cases, the forecast is either more or less than a prediction of the future.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Philip_E._Tetlock" title="Philip E. Tetlock">Philip E. Tetlock</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Superforecasting:_The_Art_and_Science_of_Prediction" class="mw-redirect" title="Superforecasting: The Art and Science of Prediction">Superforecasting: The Art and Science of Prediction</a></i>, he discusses forecasting as a method of improving the ability to make decisions. A person can become better calibrated<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> — <i>i.e.</i> having things they give 10% credence to happening 10% of the time. Or they can forecast things more confidently<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> — coming to the same conclusion but earlier. Some have claimed that forecasting is a transferable skill with benefits to other areas of discussion and decision making.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Betting" class="mw-redirect" title="Betting">Betting</a> on sports or politics is another form of forecasting. Rather than being used as advice, bettors are paid based on if they predicted correctly. While decisions might be made based on these bets (forecasts), the main motivation is generally financial.
</p><p>Finally, <a href="/wiki/Futarchy" title="Futarchy">futarchy</a> is a form of government where forecasts of the impact of government action are used to decide which actions are taken. Rather than advice, in futarchy's strongest form, the action with the best forecasted result is automatically taken.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Forecast_improvements">Forecast improvements</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Forecast improvements"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Forecast improvement projects have been operated in a number of sectors: the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a>'s Hurricane Forecast Improvement Project (HFIP) and the Wind Forecast Improvement Project sponsored by the <a href="/wiki/US_Department_of_Energy" class="mw-redirect" title="US Department of Energy">US Department of Energy</a> are examples.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In relation to supply chain management, the <a href="/wiki/DuPont_analysis" title="DuPont analysis">Du Pont model</a> has been used to show that an increase in forecast accuracy can generate increases in sales and reductions in inventory, operating expenses and commitment of working capital.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Groceries_Code_Adjudicator" title="Groceries Code Adjudicator">Groceries Code Adjudicator</a> in the United Kingdom, which regulates supply chain management practices in the groceries retail industry, has observed that all the retailers who fall within the scope of his regulation "are striving for continuous improvement in forecasting practice and activity in relation to promotions".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Categories_of_forecasting_methods">Categories of forecasting methods</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Categories of forecasting methods"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Qualitative_vs._quantitative_methods">Qualitative vs. quantitative methods</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Qualitative vs. quantitative methods"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Qualitative forecasting techniques are subjective, based on the opinion and judgment of consumers and experts; they are appropriate when past data are not available.
They are usually applied to intermediate- or long-range decisions. Examples of qualitative forecasting methods are<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> informed opinion and judgment, the <a href="/wiki/Delphi_method" title="Delphi method">Delphi method</a>, <a href="/wiki/Market_research" title="Market research">market research</a>, and historical life-cycle analogy.
</p><p>Quantitative forecasting <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_model" title="Mathematical model">models</a> are used to forecast future data as a function of past data. They are appropriate to use when past numerical data is available and when it is reasonable to assume that some of the patterns in the data are expected to continue into the future.
These methods are usually applied to short- or intermediate-range decisions. Examples of quantitative forecasting methods are<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> last period demand, simple and weighted N-Period <a href="/wiki/Moving_average" title="Moving average">moving averages</a>, simple <a href="/wiki/Exponential_smoothing" title="Exponential smoothing">exponential smoothing</a>, Poisson process model based forecasting<sup id="cite&#95;ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and multiplicative seasonal indexes. Previous research shows that different methods may lead to different level of forecasting accuracy. For example, <a href="/wiki/Group_method_of_data_handling" title="Group method of data handling">GMDH</a> neural network was found to have  better forecasting performance than the classical forecasting algorithms such as Single Exponential Smooth, Double Exponential Smooth, ARIMA and back-propagation neural network.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Relational_methods">Relational methods</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Relational methods"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Some forecasting methods try to identify the underlying factors that might influence the variable that is being forecast. For example, including information about climate patterns might improve the ability of a model to predict umbrella sales. Forecasting models often take account of regular seasonal variations. In addition to climate, such variations can also be due to holidays and customs: for example, one might predict that sales of college football apparel will be higher during the football season than during the off season.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-NahmiasOlsen2015&#95;22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NahmiasOlsen2015-22"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Several informal methods used in causal forecasting do not rely solely on the output of mathematical <a href="/wiki/Algorithm" title="Algorithm">algorithms</a>, but instead use the judgment of the forecaster. Some forecasts take account of past relationships between variables: if one variable has, for example, been approximately linearly related to another for a long period of time, it may be appropriate to extrapolate such a relationship into the future, without necessarily understanding the reasons for the relationship.
</p><p>Causal methods include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Regression_analysis" title="Regression analysis">Regression analysis</a> includes a large group of methods for predicting future values of a variable using information about other variables. These methods include both <a href="/wiki/Parametric_statistics" title="Parametric statistics">parametric</a> (linear or non-linear) and <a href="/wiki/Nonparametric_regression" title="Nonparametric regression">non-parametric</a> techniques.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/ARMAX" class="mw-redirect" title="ARMAX">Autoregressive moving average with exogenous inputs (ARMAX)</a><sup id="cite&#95;ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>23<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Quantitative forecasting models are often judged against each other by comparing their in-sample or out-of-sample <a href="/wiki/Mean_square_error" class="mw-redirect" title="Mean square error">mean square error</a>, although some researchers have advised against this.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Different forecasting approaches have different levels of accuracy. For example, it was found in one context that <a href="/wiki/GMDH" class="mw-redirect" title="GMDH">GMDH</a> has higher forecasting accuracy than traditional ARIMA.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>25<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Forecasting_accuracy">Forecasting accuracy</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Forecasting accuracy"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The forecast error (also known as a <a href="/wiki/Errors_and_residuals" title="Errors and residuals">residual</a>) is the difference between the actual value and the forecast value for the corresponding period:
</p>
<dl><dd><span class="mwe-math-element mwe-math-element-inline"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" alttext="{\displaystyle \ E_{t}=Y_{t}-F_{t}}">
  <semantics>

        <mtext>&#xA0;</mtext>
        <msub>

            <mi>t</mi>

        </msub>
        <mo>=</mo>
        <msub>
          <mi>Y</mi>
          <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
            <mi>t</mi>
          </mrow>
        </msub>
        <mo>&#x2212;<!-- − --></mo>
        <msub>
          <mi>F</mi>
          <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
            <mi>t</mi>
          </mrow>
        </msub>
      </mstyle>
    </mrow>
    <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle \ E_{t}=Y_{t}-F_{t}}</annotation>
  </semantics>
</math></span><img src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c9cd9a0cf23736318a41a86f87f3681ba70a9319" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline mw-invert skin-invert" aria-hidden="true" style="vertical-align: -0.671ex; width:13.558ex; height:2.509ex;" alt="{\displaystyle \ E_{t}=Y_{t}-F_{t}}"></span></dd></dl>
<p>where E is the forecast error at period t, Y is the actual value at period t, and F is the forecast for period t.
</p><p>A good forecasting method will yield residuals that are <b>uncorrelated</b>. If there are <a href="/wiki/Correlation" title="Correlation">correlations</a> between residual values, then there is information left in the residuals which should be used in computing forecasts. This can be accomplished by computing the expected value of a residual as a function of the known past residuals, and adjusting the forecast by the amount by which this expected value differs from zero.
</p><p>A good forecasting method will also have <b>zero mean</b>. If the residuals have a mean other than zero, then the forecasts are biased and can be improved by adjusting the forecasting technique by an additive constant that equals the mean of the unadjusted residuals.

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Training_and_test_sets">Training and test sets</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Training and test sets"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>When evaluating the quality of forecasts, it is invalid to look at how well a model fits the historical data; the accuracy of forecasts can only be determined by considering how well a model performs on new data that were not used when fitting the model. When choosing models, it is common to use a portion of the available data for fitting, and use the rest of the data for testing the model, as was done in the above examples.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-e2.5&#95;28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-e2.5-28"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>28<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Cross-validation">Cross-validation</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Cross-validation"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cross-validation_(statistics)" title="Cross-validation (statistics)">Cross-validation</a> is a more sophisticated version of training a test set.
</p><p>For <a href="/wiki/Cross-sectional_data" title="Cross-sectional data">cross-sectional data</a>, one approach to cross-validation works as follows:
</p>
<ol><li>Select observation <i>i</i> for the test set, and use the remaining observations in the training set. Compute the error on the test observation.</li>
<li>Repeat the above step for  <i>i</i> = 1,2,..., <i>N</i> where <i>N</i> is the total number of observations.</li>
<li>Compute the forecast accuracy measures based on the errors obtained.</li></ol>
<p>This makes efficient use of the available data, as only one observation is omitted at each step
</p><p>For time series data, the training set can only include observations prior to the test set. Therefore, no future observations can be used in constructing the forecast. Suppose <i>k</i> observations are needed to produce a reliable forecast; then the process works as follows:
</p>
<ol><li>Starting with <i>i</i>=1, select the observation <i>k + i</i>  for the test set, and use the observations at times  1, 2, ..., <i>k+i</i>–1 to estimate the forecasting model. Compute the error on the forecast for <i>k+i</i>.</li>
<li>Repeat the above step for <i>i</i> = 2,...,<i>T–k</i>  where <i>T</i> is the total number of observations.</li>
<li>Compute the forecast accuracy over all errors.</li></ol>
<p>This procedure is sometimes known as a "rolling forecasting origin" because the "origin" (<i>k+i -1)</i> at which the forecast is based rolls forward in time.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-e2.5&#95;28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-e2.5-28"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>28<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Further, two-step-ahead or in general <i>p</i>-step-ahead forecasts can be computed by first forecasting the value immediately after the training set, then using this value with the training set values to forecast two periods ahead, etc.
</p><p><i>See also</i>

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Seasonality_and_cyclic_behaviour">Seasonality and cyclic behaviour</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Seasonality and cyclic behaviour"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Seasonality">Seasonality</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Seasonality"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Seasonality" title="Seasonality">Seasonality</a></div>
<p>Seasonality is a characteristic of a time series in which the data experiences regular and predictable changes which recur every calendar year. Any predictable change or pattern in a time series that recurs or repeats over a one-year period can be said to be seasonal. It is common in many situations – such as grocery store<sup id="cite&#95;ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>29<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> or even in a Medical Examiner's office<sup id="cite&#95;ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>30<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>—that the demand depends on the day of the week. In such situations, the forecasting procedure calculates the seasonal index of the "season" – seven seasons, one for each day – which is the ratio of the average demand of that season (which is calculated by Moving Average or Exponential Smoothing using  historical data corresponding only to that season) to the  average demand across all seasons. An index higher than 1 indicates that demand is higher than average; an index less than 1 indicates that the demand is less than the average.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Cyclic_behaviour">Cyclic behaviour</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Cyclic behaviour"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The cyclic behaviour of data takes place when there are regular fluctuations in the data which usually last for an interval of at least two years, and when the length of the current cycle cannot be predetermined. Cyclic behavior is not to be confused with seasonal behavior. Seasonal fluctuations follow a consistent pattern each year so the period is always known. As an example, during the Christmas period, inventories of stores tend to increase in order to prepare for Christmas shoppers. As an example of cyclic behaviour, the population of a particular natural ecosystem will exhibit cyclic behaviour when the population decreases as its natural food source decreases, and once the population is low, the food source will recover and the population will start to increase again. Cyclic data cannot be accounted for using ordinary seasonal adjustment since it is not of fixed period.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Limitations">Limitations</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Limitations"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Limitations pose barriers beyond which forecasting methods cannot reliably predict. There are many events and values that cannot be forecast reliably. Events such as the roll of a die or the results of the lottery cannot be forecast because they are random events and there is no significant relationship in the data. When the factors that lead to what is being forecast are not known or well understood such as in <a href="/wiki/Stock_market" title="Stock market">stock</a> and <a href="/wiki/Foreign_exchange_market" title="Foreign exchange market">foreign exchange markets</a> forecasts are often inaccurate or wrong as there is not enough data about everything that affects these markets for the forecasts to be reliable, in addition the outcomes of the forecasts of these markets change the behavior of those involved in the market further reducing forecast accuracy.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-https&#58;//www.otexts.org/fpp/1/1&#95;4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-https://www.otexts.org/fpp/1/1-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The concept of "self-destructing predictions" concerns the way in which some predictions can undermine themselves by influencing social behavior.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:0&#95;31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-31"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>31<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> This is because "predictors are part of the social context about which they are trying to make a prediction and may influence that context in the process".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:0&#95;31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-31"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>31<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> For example, a forecast that a large percentage of a population will become HIV infected based on existing trends may cause more people to avoid risky behavior and thus reduce the HIV infection rate, invalidating the forecast (which might have remained correct if it had not been publicly known). Or, a prediction that cybersecurity will become a major issue may cause organizations to implement more security cybersecurity measures, thus limiting the issue.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Performance_limits_of_fluid_dynamics_equations">Performance limits of fluid dynamics equations</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Performance limits of fluid dynamics equations"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>As proposed by <a href="/wiki/Edward_Lorenz" class="mw-redirect" title="Edward Lorenz">Edward Lorenz</a> in 1963, long range weather forecasts, those made at a range of two weeks or more, are impossible to definitively predict the state of the atmosphere, owing to the <a href="/wiki/Chaos_theory" title="Chaos theory">chaotic nature</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Fluid_dynamics" title="Fluid dynamics">fluid dynamics</a> equations involved. Extremely small errors in the initial input, such as temperatures and winds, within numerical models double every five days.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>32<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
