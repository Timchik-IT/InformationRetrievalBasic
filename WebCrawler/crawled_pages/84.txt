evidence">empirical</a> content to economic relationships.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Pesaran&#95;1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pesaran-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> More precisely, it is "the quantitative analysis of actual economic <a href="/wiki/Phenomenon" title="Phenomenon">phenomena</a> based on the concurrent development of theory and observation, related by appropriate methods of inference."<sup id="cite&#95;ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> An introductory <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a> textbook describes econometrics as allowing economists "to sift through mountains of data to extract simple relationships."<sup id="cite&#95;ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jan_Tinbergen" title="Jan Tinbergen">Jan Tinbergen</a> is one of the two founding fathers of econometrics.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The other, <a href="/wiki/Ragnar_Frisch" title="Ragnar Frisch">Ragnar Frisch</a>, also coined the term in the sense in which it is used today.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>A basic tool for econometrics is the <a href="/wiki/Multiple_linear_regression" class="mw-redirect" title="Multiple linear regression">multiple linear regression</a> model.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Greene&#95;Econometrics&#95;–&#95;multiple&#95;linear&#95;regression&#95;model&#95;8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greene_Econometrics_–_multiple_linear_regression_model-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <i>Econometric theory</i> uses <a href="/wiki/Statistical_theory" title="Statistical theory">statistical theory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_statistics" title="Mathematical statistics">mathematical statistics</a> to evaluate and develop econometric methods.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Greene&#95;Econometrics&#95;–&#95;mathematical&#95;statistics&#95;and&#95;statistical&#95;theory&#95;9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greene_Econometrics_–_mathematical_statistics_and_statistical_theory-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Wooldridge&#95;Econometrics&#95;–&#95;mathematical&#95;statistics&#95;10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wooldridge_Econometrics_–_mathematical_statistics-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Econometricians try to find <a href="/wiki/Estimator" title="Estimator">estimators</a> that have desirable statistical properties including <a href="/wiki/Bias_of_an_estimator" title="Bias of an estimator">unbiasedness</a>, <a href="/wiki/Efficiency_(statistics)" title="Efficiency (statistics)">efficiency</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Consistent_estimator" title="Consistent estimator">consistency</a>. <i>Applied econometrics</i> uses theoretical econometrics and real-world <a href="/wiki/Economic_data" title="Economic data">data</a> for assessing economic theories, developing <a href="/wiki/Econometric_model" title="Econometric model">econometric models</a>, analysing <a href="/wiki/Economic_history" title="Economic history">economic history</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Economic_forecasting" title="Economic forecasting">forecasting</a>.
</p>

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Basic_models:_linear_regression">Basic models: linear regression</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Econometrics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Basic models: linear regression"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>A basic tool for econometrics is the <a href="/wiki/Multiple_linear_regression" class="mw-redirect" title="Multiple linear regression">multiple linear regression</a> model.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Greene&#95;Econometrics&#95;–&#95;multiple&#95;linear&#95;regression&#95;model&#95;8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greene_Econometrics_–_multiple_linear_regression_model-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In modern econometrics, other statistical tools are frequently used, but linear regression is still the most frequently used starting point for an analysis.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Greene&#95;Econometrics&#95;–&#95;multiple&#95;linear&#95;regression&#95;model&#95;8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greene_Econometrics_–_multiple_linear_regression_model-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Estimating a linear regression on two variables can be visualized as fitting a line through data points representing paired values of the independent and dependent variables.

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Theory">Theory</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Econometrics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Theory"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1320445320">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}@media print{body.ns-0 .mw-parser-output .hatnote{display:none!important}}</style><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Estimation_theory" title="Estimation theory">Estimation theory</a></div>
<p>Econometric theory uses <a href="/wiki/Statistical_theory" title="Statistical theory">statistical theory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_statistics" title="Mathematical statistics">mathematical statistics</a> to evaluate and develop econometric methods.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Greene&#95;Econometrics&#95;–&#95;mathematical&#95;statistics&#95;and&#95;statistical&#95;theory&#95;9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greene_Econometrics_–_mathematical_statistics_and_statistical_theory-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Wooldridge&#95;Econometrics&#95;–&#95;mathematical&#95;statistics&#95;10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wooldridge_Econometrics_–_mathematical_statistics-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Econometricians try to find <a href="/wiki/Estimator" title="Estimator">estimators</a> that have desirable statistical properties including <a href="/wiki/Bias_of_an_estimator" title="Bias of an estimator">unbiasedness</a>, <a href="/wiki/Efficiency_(statistics)" title="Efficiency (statistics)">efficiency</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Consistent_estimator" title="Consistent estimator">consistency</a>. An estimator is unbiased if its expected value is the true value of the <a href="/wiki/Parameter" title="Parameter">parameter</a>; it is consistent if it converges to the true value as the sample size gets larger, and it is efficient if the estimator has lower standard error than other unbiased estimators for a given sample size. <a href="/wiki/Ordinary_least_squares" title="Ordinary least squares">Ordinary least squares</a> (OLS) is often used for estimation since it provides the BLUE or "best linear unbiased estimator" (where "best" means most efficient, unbiased estimator) given the <a href="/wiki/Gauss%E2%80%93Markov_theorem" title="Gauss–Markov theorem">Gauss-Markov</a> assumptions. When these assumptions are violated or other statistical properties are desired, other estimation techniques such as <a href="/wiki/Maximum_likelihood_estimation" title="Maximum likelihood estimation">maximum likelihood estimation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Generalized_method_of_moments" title="Generalized method of moments">generalized method of moments</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Generalized_least_squares" title="Generalized least squares">generalized least squares</a> are used. <a href="/wiki/Bayes_estimator" title="Bayes estimator">Estimators that incorporate prior beliefs</a> are advocated by those who favour <a href="/wiki/Bayesian_statistics" title="Bayesian statistics">Bayesian statistics</a> over traditional, classical or <a href="/wiki/Frequentist_probability" title="Frequentist probability">"frequentist" approaches</a>.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Methods">Methods</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Econometrics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Methods"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1320445320" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Methodology_of_econometrics" title="Methodology of econometrics">Methodology of econometrics</a></div>
<p><i>Applied econometrics</i> uses theoretical econometrics and real-world <a href="/wiki/Economic_data" title="Economic data">data</a> for assessing economic theories, developing <a href="/wiki/Econometric_model" title="Econometric model">econometric models</a>, analysing <a href="/wiki/Economic_history" title="Economic history">economic history</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Economic_forecasting" title="Economic forecasting">forecasting</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Econometrics uses standard <a href="/wiki/Statistical_model" title="Statistical model">statistical models</a> to study economic questions, but most often these are based on <a href="/wiki/Observational_study" title="Observational study">observational</a> data, rather than data from <a href="/wiki/Experiment" title="Experiment">controlled experiments</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Introductory&#95;econometrics&#95;15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Introductory_econometrics-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In this, the design of observational studies in econometrics is similar to the design of studies in other observational disciplines, such as astronomy, epidemiology, sociology and political science. Analysis of data from an observational study is guided by the study protocol, although <a href="/wiki/Exploratory_data_analysis" title="Exploratory data analysis">exploratory data analysis</a> may be useful for generating new hypotheses.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Economics often analyses systems of equations and inequalities, such as <a href="/wiki/Supply_and_demand" title="Supply and demand">supply and demand</a> hypothesized to be in <a href="/wiki/Economic_equilibrium" title="Economic equilibrium">equilibrium</a>. Consequently, the field of econometrics has developed methods for <a href="/wiki/Parameter_identification_problem" title="Parameter identification problem">identification</a> and <a href="/wiki/Estimation_theory" title="Estimation theory">estimation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Simultaneous_equations_model" title="Simultaneous equations model">simultaneous equations models</a>. These methods are analogous to methods used in other areas of science, such as the field of <a href="/wiki/System_identification" title="System identification">system identification</a> in <a href="/wiki/Systems_analysis" title="Systems analysis">systems analysis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Control_theory" title="Control theory">control theory</a>. Such methods may allow researchers to estimate models and investigate their empirical consequences, without directly manipulating the system.
</p><p>In the absence of evidence from controlled experiments, econometricians often seek illuminating <a href="/wiki/Natural_experiment" title="Natural experiment">natural experiments</a> or apply <a href="/wiki/Quasi-experiment" title="Quasi-experiment">quasi-experimental methods</a> to draw credible causal inference.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The methods include <a href="/wiki/Regression_discontinuity_design" title="Regression discontinuity design">regression discontinuity design</a>, <a href="/wiki/Instrumental_variables_estimation" class="mw-redirect" title="Instrumental variables estimation">instrumental variables</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Difference_in_differences" title="Difference in differences">difference-in-differences</a>.
</p>

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Limitations_and_criticisms">Limitations and criticisms</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Econometrics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Limitations and criticisms"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Integrating statistics into economic theory to develop claims of causality lead to disagreements within the discipline, resulting in criticisms of econometrics. Most of these criticisms have been resolved as a result of the <a href="/wiki/Credibility_revolution" title="Credibility revolution">credibility revolution</a> and the improved rigor of the <a href="/wiki/Potential_outcomes" class="mw-redirect" title="Potential outcomes">potential outcomes framework</a>, used today by applied economists, microeconomists and econometricians for generating results that interpret causality. While developments by econometricians began in the mid 1960s to improve statistical measures, the publication in 2009 of <i><a href="/wiki/Mostly_Harmless_Econometrics" title="Mostly Harmless Econometrics">Mostly Harmless Econometrics</a></i> by economists <a href="/wiki/Joshua_Angrist" title="Joshua Angrist">Joshua D. Angrist</a> and <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B6rn-Steffen_Pischke" title="Jörn-Steffen Pischke">Jörn-Steffen Pischke</a> has summarized the improvements in econometric modeling. <a href="/wiki/Structural_causal_modeling" class="mw-redirect" title="Structural causal modeling">Structural causal modeling</a>, which attempts to formalize the limitations of quasi-experimental methods from a causality perspective, allowing experimenters to precisely quantify the risks of quasi-experimental research, is the primary academic response to this critique.  
</p><p>Like other forms of statistical analysis, badly specified econometric models may show a <a href="/wiki/Spurious_relationship" title="Spurious relationship">spurious relationship</a> where two variables are correlated but causally unrelated. In a study of the use of econometrics in major economics journals, <a href="/wiki/Deidre_McCloskey" class="mw-redirect" title="Deidre McCloskey">McCloskey</a> concluded that some economists report <a href="/wiki/P-value" title="P-value"><i>p</i>-values</a> (following the <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Fisher" title="Ronald Fisher">Fisherian</a> tradition of <a href="/wiki/Tests_of_significance" class="mw-redirect" title="Tests of significance">tests of significance</a> of point <a href="/wiki/Null_hypothesis" title="Null hypothesis">null-hypotheses</a>) and neglect concerns of <a href="/wiki/Type_II_error" class="mw-redirect" title="Type II error">type II errors</a>; some economists fail to report estimates of the size of effects (apart from <a href="/wiki/Statistical_significance" title="Statistical significance">statistical significance</a>) and to discuss their economic importance. She also argues that some economists also fail to use economic reasoning for <a href="/wiki/Model_selection" title="Model selection">model selection</a>, especially for deciding which variables to include in a regression.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>28<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>29<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In some cases, economic variables cannot be experimentally manipulated as treatments randomly assigned to subjects.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Leamer&#95;31–43&#95;30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Leamer_31–43-30"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>30<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In such cases, economists rely on <a href="/wiki/Observational_studies" class="mw-redirect" title="Observational studies">observational studies</a>, often using data sets with many strongly associated <a href="/wiki/Covariate" class="mw-redirect" title="Covariate">covariates</a>, resulting in enormous numbers of models with similar explanatory ability but different covariates and regression estimates. Regarding the plurality of models compatible with observational data-sets, <a href="/wiki/Edward_Leamer" class="mw-redirect" title="Edward Leamer">Edward Leamer</a> urged that "professionals ... properly withhold belief until an inference can be shown to be adequately insensitive to the choice of assumptions".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Leamer&#95;31–43&#95;30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Leamer_31–43-30"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>30<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Difficulties_in_model_specification">Difficulties in model specification</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Econometrics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Difficulties in model specification"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Like other forms of statistical analysis, badly specified econometric models may show a <a href="/wiki/Spurious_correlation" class="mw-redirect" title="Spurious correlation">spurious correlation</a> where two variables are correlated but causally unrelated. Economist <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Coase" title="Ronald Coase">Ronald Coase</a> is widely reported to have said "if you torture the data long enough it will confess".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>31<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Deirdre_McCloskey" title="Deirdre McCloskey">Deirdre McCloskey</a> argues that in published econometric work, economists often fail to use economic reasoning for including or excluding variables, equivocate statistical significance with substantial significance, and fail to report the <a href="/wiki/Power_of_a_test" class="mw-redirect" title="Power of a test"><i>power</i></a> of their findings.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>32<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Economic variables are observed in reality, and therefore are not readily isolated for experimental testing. <a href="/wiki/Edward_E._Leamer" title="Edward E. Leamer">Edward Leamer</a> argued there was no essential difference between econometric analysis and <a href="/wiki/Randomized_trial" class="mw-redirect" title="Randomized trial">randomized trials</a> or <a href="/wiki/Controlled_trials" class="mw-redirect" title="Controlled trials">controlled trials</a>, provided the use of statistical techniques reduces the specification bias, the effects of collinearity between the variables, to the same order as the uncertainty due to the sample size.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-auto&#95;33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-33"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>33<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Today, this critique is unbinding, as advances in identification are stronger. Identification today may report the average treatment effect (ATE), the average treatment effect <i>on the treated</i> (ATT), or the <i>local</i> average treatment effect (LATE).<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:1&#95;13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Specification_bias" class="mw-redirect" title="Specification bias">Specification bias</a>, or <i>selection bias</i> can be easily removed, through advances in sampling techniques and the ability to sample much larger populations through improved communications, data storage, and <a href="/wiki/Randomization" title="Randomization">randomization techniques</a>. Secondly, collinearity can easily be controlled for, through instrumental variables. By reporting either ATT or LATE we can control for or eliminate heterogenous error, reporting only the effects on the group as defined.
</p><p>Economists, when using data, may have a number of explanatory <a href="/wiki/Explanatory_variable" class="mw-redirect" title="Explanatory variable">variables</a> they want to use that are highly collinear, such that researcher bias may be important in variable selection. Leamer argues that economists can mitigate this by running statistical tests with different specified models and discarding any inferences which prove to be "fragile", concluding that "professionals ... properly withhold belief until an inference can be shown to be adequately insensitive to the choice of assumptions."<sup id="cite&#95;ref-auto&#95;33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-33"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>33<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Today, this is known as <a href="/wiki/P-hacking" class="mw-redirect" title="P-hacking">p-hacking</a>, and is not a failure of econometric methodology, but is instead a potential failure of a researcher who may be seeking to prove their own hypothesis.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>34<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> P-hacking is not accepted in economics, and the requirement to disclose original data and the code to perform statistical analysis.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>35<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> However <a href="/wiki/Sala-i-Martin" class="mw-redirect" title="Sala-i-Martin">Sala-I-Martin</a><sup id="cite&#95;ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>36<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> argued, it's possible to specify two models suggesting contrary relation between two variables. The phenomenon was labeled <i>emerging recalcitrant result</i> phenomenon by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Goldfarb" title="Robert Goldfarb">Robert Goldfarb</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>37<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> This is known as <a href="/wiki/Endogeneity_(econometrics)" title="Endogeneity (econometrics)">two-way causality</a>, and should be discussed with respect to the underlying theory that the mechanism is attempting to capture.
</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Peter_Kennedy_(economist)" title="Peter Kennedy (economist)">Kennedy</a> (1998, p 1-2) reports econometricians as being accused of <i>using sledgehammers to crack open peanuts</i>. That is they use a wide range of complex statistical techniques <i>while turning a blind eye to data deficiencies and the many questionable assumptions required for the application of these techniques</i>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>38<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Kennedy quotes <a href="/wiki/Stefan_Valavanis" class="mw-redirect" title="Stefan Valavanis">Stefan Valavanis</a>'s 1959 Econometrics textbook's critique of practice:</p><blockquote><p>Econometric theory is like an exquisitely balanced French recipe, spelling out precisely with how many turns to mix the sauce, how many carats of spice to add, and for how many milliseconds to bake the mixture at exactly 474 degrees of temperature. But when the statistical cook turns to raw materials, he finds that hearts of cactus fruit are unavailable, so he substitutes chunks of cantaloupe; where the recipe calls for vermicelli he used shredded wheat; and he substitutes green garment die for curry, ping-pong balls for turtles eggs, and for Chalifougnac vintage 1883, a can of turpentine. (1959, p.83)<sup id="cite&#95;ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>39<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></p></blockquote>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Macroeconomic_critiques">Macroeconomic critiques</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Econometrics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Macroeconomic critiques"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Looking primarily at macroeconomics, <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Summers" class="mw-redirect" title="Lawrence Summers">Lawrence Summers</a> has criticized econometric formalism, arguing that "the empirical facts of which we are most confident and which provide the most secure basis for theory are those that require the least sophisticated statistical analysis to perceive." <b>Summers is not critiquing the methodology itself</b> but instead its usefulness in developing macroeconomic theory.
</p><p>He looks at two well cited macroeconometric studies (<a href="/wiki/Lars_Peter_Hansen" title="Lars Peter Hansen">Hansen</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Singleton" title="Kenneth Singleton">Singleton</a> (1982, 1983), and <a href="/wiki/Ben_Bernanke" title="Ben Bernanke">Bernanke</a> (1986)), and argues that while both make brilliant use of econometric methods, both papers do not speak to formal theoretical proof. Noting that in the natural sciences, "investigators rush to check out the validity of claims made by rival laboratories and then build on them," Summers points out that this rarely happen in economics, which to him is a result of the fact that "the results [of econometric studies] are rarely an important input to theory creation or the evolution of professional opinion more generally." To Summers:<sup id="cite&#95;ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>40<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1244412712">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 32px}.mw-parser-output .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;margin-top:0}@media(min-width:500px){.mw-parser-output .templatequotecite{padding-left:1.6em}}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Successful empirical research has been characterized by attempts to gauge the strength of associations rather than to estimate structural parameters, verbal characterizations of how causal relations might operate rather than explicit mathematical models, and the skillful use of carefully chosen natural experiments rather than sophisticated statistical technique to achieve identification.</p></blockquote>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading4"><h4 id="Lucas_critique">Lucas critique</h4><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Econometrics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Lucas critique"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1320445320" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lucas_critique" title="Lucas critique">Lucas critique</a></div>
<p>Robert Lucas criticised the use of overly simplistic econometric models of the macroeconomy to predict the implications of <a href="/wiki/Economic_policy" title="Economic policy">economic policy</a>, arguing that the structural relationships observed in historical models break down if decision makers adjust their preferences to reflect policy changes. Lucas argued that policy conclusions drawn from contemporary <a href="/wiki/Large-scale_macroeconometric_model" title="Large-scale macroeconometric model">large-scale macroeconometric models</a> were invalid as economic actors would change their expectations of the future and adjust their behaviour accordingly. Good macroeconometric model should incorporate <a href="/wiki/Microfoundations" title="Microfoundations">microfoundations</a> to model the effects of policy change, with equations representing economic <a href="/wiki/Agent_(economics)" title="Agent (economics)">representative agents</a> responding to economic changes based on <a href="/wiki/Rational_expectations" title="Rational expectations">rational expectations</a> of the future; implying their pattern of behaviour might be quite different if economic policy changed.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Austrian_School_critique">Austrian School critique</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Econometrics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Austrian School critique"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The current-day <a href="/wiki/Austrian_School" class="mw-redirect" title="Austrian School">Austrian School of Economics</a> typically rejects much of econometric modeling. The historical data used to make econometric models, they claim, represents behavior under circumstances idiosyncratic to the past; thus econometric models show correlational, not causal, relationships. Econometricians have addressed this criticism by adopting quasi-experimental methodologies. Austrian school economists remain skeptical of these corrected models, continuing in their belief that statistical methods are unsuited for the social sciences.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>41<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The Austrian School holds that the counterfactual must be known for a causal relationship to be established. The changes due to the counterfactual could then be extracted from the observed changes, leaving only the changes caused by the variable. Meeting this critique is very challenging since "there is no dependable method for ascertaining the uniquely correct counterfactual" for historical data.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>42<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> For non-historical data, the Austrian critique is met with <a href="/wiki/Randomized_controlled_trial" title="Randomized controlled trial">randomized controlled trials</a>. Randomized controlled trials must be purposefully prepared, which historical data is not.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:0a&#95;43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0a-43"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>43<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The use of randomized controlled trials is becoming more common in social science research. In the United States, for example, the Education Sciences Reform Act of 2002 made funding for education research contingent on scientific validity defined in part as "experimental designs using random assignment, when feasible."<sup id="cite&#95;ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>44<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In answering questions of causation, parametric statistics only addresses the Austrian critique in randomized controlled trials.
</p><p>If the data is not from a randomized controlled trial, econometricians meet the Austrian critique with <a href="/wiki/Quasi-experiment" title="Quasi-experiment">quasi-experimental</a> methodologies, including identifying and exploiting <a href="/wiki/Natural_experiment" title="Natural experiment">natural experiments</a>. These methodologies attempt to extract the counterfactual post-hoc so that the use of the tools of parametric statistics is justified. Since parametric statistics depends on any observation following a Gaussian distribution, which is only guaranteed by the <a href="/wiki/Central_limit_theorem" title="Central limit theorem">central limit theorem</a> in a randomization methodology, the use of tools such as the confidence interval will be outside of their specification: the amount of selection bias will always be unknown.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>45<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
