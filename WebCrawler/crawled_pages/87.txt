<p><b>Investment</b> is traditionally defined as the "commitment of resources into something expected to gain value over time".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> If an investment involves money, then it can be defined as a "commitment of money to receive more money later". From a broader viewpoint, an investment can be defined as "to tailor the pattern of expenditure and receipt of resources to optimise the desirable patterns of these flows". When expenditures and receipts are defined in terms of money, then the net monetary receipt in a time period is termed <a href="/wiki/Cash_flow" title="Cash flow">cash flow</a>, while money received in a series of several time periods is termed cash flow stream.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Finance" title="Finance">finance</a>, the purpose of investing is to generate a <a href="/wiki/Return_(finance)" class="mw-redirect" title="Return (finance)">return</a> on the invested <a href="/wiki/Asset" title="Asset">asset</a>. The return may consist of a capital gain (profit) or loss, realised if the  investment is sold, unrealised <a href="/wiki/Capital_appreciation" title="Capital appreciation">capital appreciation</a> (or depreciation) if yet unsold. It may also consist of periodic income such as <a href="/wiki/Dividend" title="Dividend">dividends</a>, <a href="/wiki/Interest" title="Interest">interest</a>, or rental income. The return may also include currency gains or losses due to changes in foreign currency <a href="/wiki/Exchange_rate" title="Exchange rate">exchange rates</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Investor" title="Investor">Investors</a> generally expect higher <a href="/wiki/Rate_of_return" title="Rate of return">returns</a> from <a href="/wiki/Financial_risk" title="Financial risk">riskier</a> investments. When a low-risk investment is made, the return is also generally low. Similarly, high risk comes with a chance of high losses. Investors, particularly novices, are often advised to <a href="/wiki/Diversification_(finance)" title="Diversification (finance)">diversify</a> their <a href="/wiki/Portfolio_(finance)" title="Portfolio (finance)">portfolio</a>. Diversification has the <a href="/wiki/Statistics" title="Statistics">statistical</a> effect of reducing overall risk.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Types_of_financial_investments">Types of financial investments</h2></div>
<p>In modern economies, <a href="/wiki/Traditional_investments" title="Traditional investments">traditional investments</a> include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Stock" title="Stock">Stocks</a> – <a href="/wiki/Business" title="Business">Business</a> ownership, known as <a href="/wiki/Equity_(finance)" title="Equity (finance)">equity</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Publicly_traded_companies" class="mw-redirect" title="Publicly traded companies">publicly traded companies</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bond_(finance)" title="Bond (finance)">Bonds</a> – <a href="/wiki/Loan" title="Loan">loans</a> to governments and businesses traded on public markets</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cash" title="Cash">Cash</a> – holding a particular <a href="/wiki/Currency" title="Currency">currency</a>, whether in anticipation of spending or to take advantage of or hedge against changes in a <a href="/wiki/Currency_exchange_rate" class="mw-redirect" title="Currency exchange rate">currency exchange rate</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Real_estate_investing" title="Real estate investing">Real estate</a>, which can be rented to provide ongoing income or resold if it increases in value</li></ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/Alternative_investment" title="Alternative investment">Alternative investments</a> include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Private_equity" title="Private equity">Private equity</a> in businesses that are not publicly traded on a <a href="/wiki/Stock_exchange" title="Stock exchange">stock exchange</a>, often involving <a href="/wiki/Venture_capital" title="Venture capital">venture capital</a> funds, <a href="/wiki/Angel_investor" title="Angel investor">angel investors</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Equity_crowdfunding" title="Equity crowdfunding">equity crowdfunding</a></li>
<li>Other <a href="/wiki/Loan" title="Loan">loans</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Mortgage" title="Mortgage">mortgages</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Commodities" class="mw-redirect" title="Commodities">Commodities</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Precious_metal" title="Precious metal">precious metals</a> like <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a>, <a href="/wiki/Agricultural" class="mw-redirect" title="Agricultural">agricultural</a> products like <a href="/wiki/Potatoes" class="mw-redirect" title="Potatoes">potatoes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> deliveries like <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Collectables" class="mw-redirect" title="Collectables">Collectables</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Art" title="Art">art</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coin" title="Coin">coins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vintage_car" title="Vintage car">vintage cars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Postage_stamp" title="Postage stamp">postage stamps</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">wine</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Carbon_offsets_and_credits" title="Carbon offsets and credits">Carbon offsets and credits</a></li>
<li>Digital entities like <a href="/wiki/Cryptocurrency" title="Cryptocurrency">cryptocurrency</a> and <a href="/wiki/Non-fungible_token" title="Non-fungible token">non-fungible tokens</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hedge_fund" title="Hedge fund">Hedge funds</a> that use sophisticated techniques like:
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Derivative_(finance)" title="Derivative (finance)">Derivatives</a>, the value of which is determined by a contract and is derived by calculation from the performance of some other sort of underlying investment; these include <a href="/wiki/Forward_contract" title="Forward contract">forwards</a>, <a href="/wiki/Futures_contract" title="Futures contract">futures</a>, <a href="/wiki/Option_(finance)" title="Option (finance)">options</a>, <a href="/wiki/Swap_(finance)" title="Swap (finance)">swaps</a>, <a href="/wiki/Collateralized_debt_obligation" title="Collateralized debt obligation">collateralized debt obligations</a>, <a href="/wiki/Credit_default_swap" title="Credit default swap">credit default swaps</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tax_Receivable_Agreement" title="Tax Receivable Agreement">Tax Receivable Agreements</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Leverage_(finance)" title="Leverage (finance)">Leveraged investing</a>, which is the investment of borrowed money</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Short_selling" class="mw-redirect" title="Short selling">Short selling</a>, which typically uses leverage and derivatives to bet that the value of a stock will decline</li></ul></li></ul>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Investment_and_risk">Investment and risk</h2></div>
<p>An investor may bear a <a href="/wiki/Financial_risk" title="Financial risk">risk</a> of loss of some or all of their <a href="/wiki/Financial_capital" title="Financial capital">capital</a> invested. Investment differs from <a href="/wiki/Arbitrage" title="Arbitrage">arbitrage</a>, in which <a href="/wiki/Profit_(accounting)" title="Profit (accounting)">profit</a> is generated without investing capital or bearing risk.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Saving" title="Saving">Savings</a> bear the (normally remote) risk that the financial provider may default.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Foreign_currency" class="mw-redirect" title="Foreign currency">Foreign currency</a> savings also bear <a href="/wiki/Foreign_exchange_risk" title="Foreign exchange risk">foreign exchange risk</a>: if the currency of a savings account differs from the account holder's home currency, then there is the risk that the exchange rate between the two currencies will move unfavourably so that the value of the savings account decreases, measured in the account holder's home currency.
</p><p>Even investing in tangible assets like property has its risk. And similar to most risks, property buyers can seek to mitigate any potential risk by taking out mortgage and by borrowing at a lower loan to security ratio.
</p><p>In contrast with savings, investments tend to carry more risk, in the form of both a wider variety of risk factors and a greater level of uncertainty.
</p><p>Industry to industry volatility is more or less of a risk depending. In <a href="/wiki/Biotechnology" title="Biotechnology">biotechnology</a>, for example, investors look for big profits on companies that have small market capitalizations but can be worth hundreds of millions quite quickly. The risk is high because approximately 90% of biotechnology products researched do not make it to market due to regulations and the complex demands within pharmacology as the average prescription drug takes 10 years and US$2.5 billion worth of capital.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="History">History</h2></div>
<p>Early forms of credit and trade-based lending appeared in ancient Mesopotamia, supporting commercial activity across the region. More formal regulation emerges in the Code of Hammurabi, compiled around 1754 BCE, which includes several provisions governing loans, collateral, maximum interest rates, and the rights of creditors and debtors—indicating the presence of structured, investment-like arrangements in ancient Babylon.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> 
</p><p>In ancient Rome, financial intermediaries known as <i>argentarii</i> and <i>nummularii</i> provided credit, accepted deposits, facilitated commercial payments, and mediated currency exchange. Their activities demonstrate the existence of organized financial intermediation and early investment-related services within the Roman imperial economy.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>During the medieval period, merchant banking families in Italian city-states such as Florence, Genoa, and Venice developed early deposit, credit, and bill-of-exchange practices that supported expanding European and Mediterranean trade networks. These institutions laid the commercial and legal foundations for later financial markets.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>These early financial practices eventually contributed to the more structured investment systems that emerged in Europe during the early modern period. By the 17th century, the growth of global trade networks made investment activity increasingly recognizable in its modern form. Shipping ventures to Asia undertaken by Dutch, British, and French companies often required large pools of capital, encouraging shipowners to seek outside investors willing to finance long-distance voyages in exchange for a share of the profits when ships returned safely.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>A major development in this period was the founding of the Amsterdam Stock Exchange in 1602, often considered the world’s first modern securities exchange. It was created to support trading in shares of the Dutch East India Company (Vereenigde Oost-Indische Compagnie or VOC), the first company to issue publicly traded stock. Amsterdam’s financial infrastructure—including an Exchange Bank to stabilize currency payments and merchant banks that facilitated regulated trade—helped establish the city as a global center of commerce and capital during the 17th century.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The origins of pooled investment vehicles trace back to the late 18th century in the Netherlands, when the first known investment trust — created by Dutch businessman Adriaan van Ketwich — allowed small investors to combine capital, thereby diversifying risk across a portfolio of assets.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In the United States, the origins of the stock market trace back to May 17, 1792, when 24 brokers signed the Buttonwood Agreement, establishing rules for trading securities among trusted parties. A few years earlier, the Compromise of 1790 had allowed Alexander Hamilton to consolidate Revolutionary War debts through federally issued bonds, effectively creating the first widely traded securities market in America.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The New York Stock and Exchange Board—later renamed the New York Stock Exchange (NYSE)—was formally organized in 1817, meeting twice daily to trade a small list of stocks and bonds. The exchange grew rapidly over the 19th century, and by the end of the Civil War in 1865, more than 300 securities were being actively traded, marking the emergence of a mature and organized American securities market.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Investment_strategies">Investment strategies</h2></div>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Value_investing">Value investing</h3></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1320445320" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Value_investing" title="Value investing">Value investing</a></div>
<p>A value investor buys assets that they believe to be undervalued (and sells overvalued ones). To identify undervalued securities, a value investor uses analysis of the financial reports of the issuer to evaluate the security. Value investors employ accounting ratios, such as <a href="/wiki/Earnings_per_share" title="Earnings per share">earnings per share</a> and sales growth, to identify securities trading at prices below their worth.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Warren_Buffett" title="Warren Buffett">Warren Buffett</a> and <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Graham" title="Benjamin Graham">Benjamin Graham</a> are notable examples of value investors. Graham and <a href="/wiki/David_Dodd" title="David Dodd">Dodd's</a> seminal work, <a href="/wiki/Security_Analysis_(book)" title="Security Analysis (book)"><i>Security Analysis</i></a>, was written in the wake of the <a href="/wiki/Wall_Street_Crash_of_1929" class="mw-redirect" title="Wall Street Crash of 1929">Wall Street Crash of 1929</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Price_to_earnings_ratio" class="mw-redirect" title="Price to earnings ratio">price to earnings ratio</a> (P/E), or earnings multiple, is a particularly significant and recognized fundamental ratio, with a function of dividing the share price of the stock, by its earnings per share. This will provide the value representing the sum investors are prepared to expend for each dollar of company earnings. This ratio is an important aspect, due to its capacity as measurement for the comparison of valuations of various companies. A stock with a lower P/E ratio will cost less per share than one with a higher P/E, taking into account the same level of <a href="/wiki/Financial" class="mw-redirect" title="Financial">financial</a> performance; therefore, it essentially means a low P/E is the preferred option.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>An instance in which the price to earnings ratio has a lesser significance is when companies in different industries are compared. For example, although it is reasonable for a telecommunications stock to show a P/E in the low teens, in the case of hi-tech stock, a P/E in the 40s range is not unusual. When making comparisons, the P/E ratio can give you a refined view of a particular stock valuation.
</p><p>For investors paying for each dollar of a company's earnings, the P/E ratio is a significant indicator, but the <a href="/wiki/Price-to-book_ratio" class="mw-redirect" title="Price-to-book ratio">price-to-book ratio</a> (P/B) is also a reliable indication of how much investors are willing to spend on each dollar of company assets. In the process of the P/B ratio, the share price of a stock is divided by its net assets; any intangibles, such as goodwill, are not taken into account. It is a crucial factor of the price-to-book ratio, due to it indicating the actual payment for tangible assets and not the more difficult valuation of intangibles. Accordingly, the P/B could be considered a comparatively conservative metric.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Growth_investing">Growth investing</h3></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Growth_investing" title="Growth investing">Growth investors</a> seek investments they believe are likely to have higher earnings or greater value in the future. To identify such <a href="/wiki/Stock" title="Stock">stocks</a>, growth investors often evaluate measures of current stock value as well as predictions of future financial performance.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:1&#95;18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Growth investors seek profits through <a href="/wiki/Capital_appreciation" title="Capital appreciation">capital appreciation</a> – the gains earned when a stock is sold at a higher price than what it was purchased for. The <a href="/wiki/Price%E2%80%93earnings_ratio" title="Price–earnings ratio">price-to-earnings (P/E)</a> multiple is also used for this type of investment; growth stock are likely to have a P/E higher than others in its industry.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> According to Investopedia author Troy Segal and U.S. Department of State Fulbright fintech research awardee Julius Mansa, growth investing is best suited for investors who prefer relatively shorter investment horizons, higher risks, and are not seeking immediate cash flow through dividends.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:1&#95;18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Some investors attribute the introduction of the growth investing strategy to investment banker Thomas Rowe Price Jr., who tested and popularized the method in 1950 by introducing his <a href="/wiki/Mutual_fund" title="Mutual fund">mutual fund</a>, the T. Rowe Price Growth Stock Fund. Price asserted that investors could reap high returns by "investing in companies that are well-managed in fertile fields."<sup id="cite&#95;ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>A new form of investing that seems to have caught the attention of investors is Venture Capital. Venture Capital is independently managed dedicated pools of capital that focus on equity or equity-linked investments in privately held, high growth companies.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Momentum_investing">Momentum investing</h3></div>
<p>Momentum investors generally seek to buy stocks that are currently experiencing a short-term uptrend, and they usually sell them once this momentum starts to decrease. Stocks or <a href="/wiki/Security_(finance)" title="Security (finance)">securities</a> purchased for momentum investing are often characterized by demonstrating consistently high returns for the past three to twelve months.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> However, in a <a href="/wiki/Bear_Market" class="mw-redirect" title="Bear Market">bear market</a>, momentum investing also involves short-selling securities of stocks that are experiencing a downward trend, because it is believed that these stocks will continue to decrease in value. Essentially, momentum investing generally relies on the principle that a consistently up-trending stock will continue to grow, while a consistently down-trending stock will continue to fall.
</p><p>Economists and financial analysts have not reached a consensus on the effectiveness of using the momentum investing strategy. Rather than evaluating a company's operational performance, momentum investors instead utilize trend lines, moving averages, and the <a href="/wiki/Average_directional_movement_index" title="Average directional movement index">Average Directional Index (ADX)</a> to determine the existence and strength of trends.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:02&#95;23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:02-23"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>23<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Dollar_cost_averaging">Dollar cost averaging</h3></div>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:WikiGraphic.PNG" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/WikiGraphic.PNG/250px-WikiGraphic.PNG" decoding="async" width="250" height="149" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/WikiGraphic.PNG/500px-WikiGraphic.PNG 1.5x" data-file-width="2081" data-file-height="1243" /></a><figcaption>Dollar cost averaging: If an individual invested $500 per month into the stock market for 40 years at a 10% annual return rate, they would have an ending balance of over $2.5 million.</figcaption></figure>
<p><a href="/wiki/Dollar_cost_averaging" title="Dollar cost averaging">Dollar cost averaging</a> (DCA), also known in the UK as pound-cost averaging, is the process of consistently investing a certain amount of money across regular increments of time, and the method can be used in conjunction with value investing, growth investing, momentum investing, or other strategies. For example, an investor who practices dollar-cost averaging could choose to invest $200 a month for the next 3 years, regardless of the share price of their preferred stock(s), <a href="/wiki/Mutual_fund" title="Mutual fund">mutual funds</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Exchange-traded_fund" title="Exchange-traded fund">exchange-traded funds</a>.
</p><p>Many investors believe that dollar-cost averaging helps minimize short-term volatility by spreading risk out across time intervals and avoiding market timing.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:02&#95;23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:02-23"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>23<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Research also shows that DCA can help reduce the total average cost per share in an investment because the method enables the purchase of more shares when their price is lower, and less shares when the price is higher.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:02&#95;23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:02-23"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>23<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> However, dollar-cost averaging is also generally characterized by more brokerage fees, which could decrease an investor's overall returns.
</p><p>The term "dollar-cost averaging" is believed to have first been coined in 1949 by economist and author Benjamin Graham in his book, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Intelligent_Investor" title="The Intelligent Investor">The Intelligent Investor</a>.</i> Graham asserted that investors that use DCA are "likely to end up with a satisfactory overall price for all [their] holdings."<sup id="cite&#95;ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Micro-investing">Micro-investing</h3></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Micro-investing" title="Micro-investing">Micro-investing</a> is a type of investment strategy that is designed to make investing regular, accessible and affordable, especially for those who may not have a lot of money to invest or who are new to investing.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>25<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>26<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Intermediaries_and_collective_investments">Intermediaries and collective investments</h2></div>
<p>Investments are often made indirectly through <a href="/wiki/Intermediary" title="Intermediary">intermediary</a> financial institutions. These intermediaries include <a href="/wiki/Pension_fund" title="Pension fund">pension funds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bank" title="Bank">banks</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Insurance" title="Insurance">insurance</a> companies. They may pool money received from a number of individual end investors into funds such as <a href="/wiki/Investment_trust" title="Investment trust">investment trusts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Unit_trust" title="Unit trust">unit trusts</a>, and <a href="/wiki/SICAV" title="SICAV">SICAVs</a> to make large-scale investments. Each individual investor holds an indirect or direct claim on the assets purchased, subject to charges levied by the intermediary, which may be large and varied.
</p><p>Approaches to investment sometimes referred to in marketing of collective investments include <a href="/wiki/Dollar_cost_averaging" title="Dollar cost averaging">dollar cost averaging</a> and <a href="/wiki/Market_timing" title="Market timing">market timing</a>.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Investment_valuation">Investment valuation</h2></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1320445320" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Valuation_(finance)" title="Valuation (finance)">Valuation (finance)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Business_valuation" title="Business valuation">Business valuation</a></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1320445320" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Valuation_using_discounted_cash_flows" title="Valuation using discounted cash flows">Valuation using discounted cash flows</a> and <a href="/wiki/Valuation_using_multiples" title="Valuation using multiples">Valuation using multiples</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Free_cash_flow" title="Free cash flow">Free cash flow</a> measures the cash a company generates which is available to its debt and equity investors, after allowing for reinvestment in <a href="/wiki/Working_capital" title="Working capital">working capital</a> and <a href="/wiki/Capital_expenditure" title="Capital expenditure">capital expenditure</a>. It is often used by investors as a way of measuring profitability of the company. High and rising free cash flow, therefore, tend to make a company more attractive to investors. Free cash flow can be attractive to investors because having high free cash flow can be a good indicator for high dividend or interest payments.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>27<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Debt-to-equity_ratio" title="Debt-to-equity ratio">debt-to-equity ratio</a> is an indicator of <a href="/wiki/Capital_structure" title="Capital structure">capital structure</a>. Debt-to-equity ratio measures how much debt is used to finance a company, compared to equity. A high debt-to-equity ratio means that a company relies more on debt to finance operations, and is much riskier to investors.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>28<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> A high proportion of <a href="/wiki/Debt" title="Debt">debt</a>, reflected in a high debt-to-equity ratio, tends to make a company's <a href="/wiki/Earnings" title="Earnings">earnings</a>, free cash flow, and ultimately the returns to its investors, riskier or <a href="/wiki/Volatility_(finance)" title="Volatility (finance)">volatile</a>. Investors compare a company's debt-to-equity ratio with those of other companies in the same industry, and examine trends in debt-to-equity ratios and free cashflow.
</p><p>Earnings per share (EPS) is another way to evaluate a stock and its profitability. Earnings per share is measured by dividing the net income of a company by the total number of outstanding shares. A higher earnings per share is attractive to investors because it typically means the company is more profitable. EPS shows how much money a company makes for each share of its stocks.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>29<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
